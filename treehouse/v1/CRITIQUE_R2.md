# Tree House V1 — Cinematic Immersive
## Round 2 Critique · Copy, Narrative Voice, Storytelling

Reviewing the copy in `treehouse/v1/index.html` against the brief's voice anchors (Largo restraint, botanical-Michelin-adults-only positioning) and against the sibling Boutique V1 voice. R1 fixed the visual hierarchy and the worst Spanish phrasing; R2 is about whether the words on screen actually sound like one writer who has lived in this house.

The short version: the hero, sanctuary, breath quote, and pull quotes are written by someone with a real voice. The Michelin section, rooms, art manifesto, and footer slip into three or four other voices — corporate, gallery-fair, travel-supplement, hotel-boilerplate. The reader feels the seams.

13 findings. Numbered, with quotes / why / fix.

---

### 1. CRITICAL — The hero promise and the Sanctuary opening are written by one writer; the rest of the page is written by four.

**What.** Compare these four openers, each the first body line a reader sees in a section:

- Hero tagline: *"Where the courtyard breathes. Fifteen rooms. One canopy."*
- Sanctuary body: *"Fifteen rooms. One canopy. A colonial house in Santa Ana, smothered in the kind of greenery that cools the air ten degrees before you've finished breathing in."*
- Michelin deck: *"In 2025 the Michelin Guide awarded its inaugural Keys — Michelin's first acknowledgment that some hotels deserve the same scrutiny as the kitchens they've spent a century rating."*
- Art manifesto: *"Treehouse began as a curatorial idea — a way of letting contemporary work live inside spaces with a heartbeat instead of vitrines."*
- Journal intro: *"Field guides written by people who actually live here. Updated when something is worth telling."*
- Curated intro: *"Four ways to step into the city — designed in-house, taken at your pace."*

The first two are one writer. The Michelin deck is a press release ("inaugural Keys", "first acknowledgment", "century"). The art manifesto is a gallery-fair statement ("curatorial idea", "spaces with a heartbeat"). The journal intro is the writer again. The curated intro is hotel-website-template.

**Why.** Largo's voice works because *every line sounds like the same person noticed something*. Tree House's writer-voice exists — it's in the hero, the no-front-desk line, the breath quote, the field-journal phrasing, the room summaries. But the moment a section *needs* to do positioning work (Michelin, art, curated, offers), the voice swaps for an institutional register. The reader senses a different person took the keyboard.

**Fix.** A single voice pass with one rule: *the writer is the person who knows the house, not the hotel's marketing department.* Practically:

- Michelin deck rewrite: *"Michelin began handing out Keys in 2025 — finally rating the rooms the way they've rated the kitchens for a century. Of the hotels in Mérida, they named ours."* (Same facts, dropped "inaugural Keys", "first acknowledgment", "scrutiny".)
- Art manifesto rewrite (opening): *"Treehouse started as a question: where does contemporary work feel less like an exhibit and more like a house guest. The answer, we decided, was this house."*
- Curated intro rewrite: *"Four things we do, made for the pace we keep."*

### 2. CRITICAL — The Michelin section earns the badge with stature, not with prose.

**What.** The body inside the Michelin section, after the headline:

> *"In 2025 the Michelin Guide awarded its inaugural Keys — Michelin's first acknowledgment that some hotels deserve the same scrutiny as the kitchens they've spent a century rating. Tree House was the only hotel in Mérida named."*

Then three cells:
1. *"A property of real character. The Key is given for personality, not for amenities. A house with a point of view."*
2. *"Architecture, service, & place. All three judged together. None of them louder than the others."*
3. *"First in Mérida. One in the city. The 2025 guide named exactly one property in the Yucatán capital. This one."*

Every line is *about* the Key, the criteria, the rating, the city. Not one line is about *this house*. What did the inspectors notice? What is the personality the Key was given for? The brief explicitly asks this section to feel "earned through substantive prose" — right now the prose is criteria-recitation.

**Why.** This is the prestige moment. It's the page's single biggest trust signal. Telling the reader "Michelin rates personality" and "one was named in Mérida" is structurally a *brag without a story.* Compare to how a Largo-voice writer would do it: name the thing the inspector saw. The shadow on the courtyard wall at 6pm. The way the housekeeper carries flowers. The detail that *would have failed* the rating, that you decided to keep anyway.

**Fix.** Replace at least one of the three cells with a *concrete observation from the house*. E.g.:

> *N° 02 — What they noticed. The cook brings coffee out before you ask. The bath fills from a brass tap that takes its time. The leaves above the courtyard are not pruned. We were told these were the things that made the Key — the lack of hurry.*

Strip the "all three judged together / none of them louder" line; it sounds like a Michelin spokesperson, not the writer of the hero. Keep the "First in Mérida. One in the city." cell — that one's the right voice. Reduce three cells to two and let them say something *only this house* could say.

### 3. MAJOR — The rooms' one-line voices read interchangeably, except the Penthouse.

**What.** The four room summaries side by side:

- King Garden Room: *"The smallest key. Courtyard-facing, leaf-shaded, slow to wake."*
- Queen Courtyard Suite: *"Two queens, a private terrace, a slow morning ritual."*
- Grand Foliage Suite: *"The house's principal room. A claw-foot bath under a vaulted ceiling."*
- Penthouse Canopy: *"The top of the tree. Private rooftop, plunge pool, the whole Mérida sky."*

The King has a voice ("slow to wake"). The Penthouse has a voice ("the top of the tree", "the whole Mérida sky"). The middle two are spec sheets in sentence form — *"two queens, a private terrace"* and *"a claw-foot bath under a vaulted ceiling"* could be amenity bullets on any boutique site.

**Why.** The brief calls these four "in-character" names; the names *promise* four distinct rooms with personalities. The names deliver that promise; the summaries don't. The reader who's deciding between Queen Courtyard and Grand Foliage gets no help from the prose — they're both "a nice room with some greenery." A 15-room hotel needs every room to feel like a separate choice.

**Fix.** Each room summary should be one sentence that *only that room could honestly say.* Suggestions (keep the brevity, shift the angle):

- King Garden Room: *"The smallest key, and the one that learns your morning first — the courtyard light arrives at this window before the others."* (Or keep current — it works.)
- Queen Courtyard Suite: *"Two queens, a terrace the size of a small living room, and the courtyard murmuring three floors below. The one we put friends in."*
- Grand Foliage Suite: *"The principal room. A claw-foot bath in the middle of the floor, leaves on three sides, and a ceiling vaulted high enough to lose a conversation in."*
- Penthouse Canopy: *"The top of the tree. A private rooftop, a plunge pool, and the whole Mérida sky between you and anyone who isn't here."* (Tighten current.)

Pick *one* sensory detail per room (light, sound, ceiling, sky) and let it carry.

### 4. MAJOR — The "Las Voces" testimonials are the strongest writing on the page and read as fabricated.

**What.** Five testimonials, attributed to "C & J · Berlin", "Sébastien · Paris", "M · Mexico City", "Iris · Copenhagen", "Travel Diary · 2026". Two examples:

> *"We came for four nights and lost track of the days by the second. The courtyard does most of the work — the leaves do the rest."* — C & J · Berlin

> *"The Penthouse Canopy at sunset, and the cicadas exactly as loud as a room of two people should be."* — M · Mexico City

These are *beautifully* written. The "cicadas exactly as loud as a room of two people should be" line is publishable. The "that is not luxury — that is attention" line is publishable. The "the first hotel I have stayed in where I believed the Michelin Key" line is publishable.

Therein lies the problem. Real guests don't write like this. Five guests in a row writing like this is *the writer's voice in five different jackets*. The signature Spanish kicker — *"Las Voces — Notas escritas al partir"* (notes written on leaving) — frames them as collected guest notes; but the prose is the hero's writer, doing five voices.

**Why.** This is the brief's "fabricated voice" failure mode in its purest form. The whole page's credibility depends on the reader trusting the voice. Real testimonials are uneven, idiomatic to each writer, sometimes blunt, sometimes funny, sometimes have a typo. These five are calibrated. A reader who's read four luxury hotel sites this week will smell it.

**Fix.** Two paths, pick one:

- **(a) Reframe as the writer's voice openly.** Drop attributions to invented guests. Re-section as *"Notas del libro de huéspedes — selected by the house"* or *"Five lines we kept."* Acknowledge editorial curation. The lines stay; the false-attribution stops.
- **(b) Get real ones.** Pull verbatim from actual reviews (with permission). Keep two of the five current ones as "writer's preferred lines" but mark them as such — *"From the comment book, March."* Let three be flatter, more particular, more clearly written by a non-writer. The unevenness is the credibility.

Either works. The current path — five publishable lines attributed to invented initials — is the riskiest.

### 5. MAJOR — The Spanish is decorative, not bilingual.

**What.** Spanish appears as eyebrows and one footer line:
- *El Refugio Botánico* · *La Llave Michelin* · *Las Habitaciones* · *La Rotación Actual* · *El Diario* · *La Mérida Curada* · *Las Voces — Notas escritas al partir* · *El Sitio* · *Las Ofertas*
- Footer: *"Una casa bajo el dosel. Quince llaves. Sólo adultos."*
- Interstitial: *"Una casa que respira hojas."* — A house that breathes leaves.
- One room rotation title: *Bajo el Dosel* (glossed).

Every Spanish phrase is an *all-caps eyebrow label* or a single-line poetic interstitial. There is no *running Spanish prose anywhere on the page.* A Mexican reader visiting a hotel in their own city — Santa Ana, Mérida — gets exactly: ten short titles, three full sentences, and an English manifesto.

**Why.** The brief calls for *bilingual respect for local readers* in Santa Ana / Mérida. The current Spanish is wallpaper — a "Mexican flavor" filter on an English site, not a genuinely bilingual presentation. Compare what it would mean if the no-front-desk paragraph, or one of the journal excerpts, or one testimonial, were in Spanish. The page would feel like it belonged to the city. Right now it feels like it's *about* the city.

Also: *La Mérida Curada* is a coinage. Native Spanish would more likely be *Mérida a medida* ("Mérida tailored") or *La Mérida Nuestra* ("Our Mérida") or simply *Mérida, a tu medida*. *La Mérida Curada* sounds like translated English and it sits over the most marketing-driven section.

**Fix.** Three concrete moves:

- Translate one full paragraph into Spanish — the no-front-desk paragraph is the strongest candidate, and it would be the page's most-quoted line in any language. Put both versions side by side, or alternate them by viewport — let Mexican readers see real prose in their language.
- Rename *La Mérida Curada* to *Mérida a tu medida* or *Nuestra Mérida.*
- *Las Voces — Notas escritas al partir* is good; leave it. *Las Ofertas* is fine. *El Sitio* is correct but flat — consider *El Sitio · Calle 43* to make it specific.
- One journal entry should have a Spanish-language pull quote inside the excerpt, not glossed. (The mercado entry is the natural candidate.)

### 6. MAJOR — The Art Collaboration manifesto is the most institutional voice on the page.

**What.** The manifesto:

> *"Treehouse began as a curatorial idea — a way of letting contemporary work live inside spaces with a heartbeat instead of vitrines. We partnered with SoHo Galleries in Mérida, and we started in this house, because this house already moved that way. Drawings in the corridors, ceramics on the courtyard ledge, photography at the foot of the stairs. The collection rotates four times a year. The rooms are not its backdrop."*

The final two sentences ("Drawings in the corridors... The rooms are not its backdrop") are the writer's voice. The first three sentences are gallery-fair statement — *"curatorial idea", "spaces with a heartbeat instead of vitrines", "this house already moved that way"* — which is the kind of phrase that sounds meaningful and means nothing on inspection. "Moved that way" doing what? Spaces don't move.

The Treehouse-namesake framing is also under-earned in the prose: the deck says *"The collaboration is named for this house. The collection sleeps where you do."* That's strong. The manifesto then dilutes it with abstraction instead of *showing* what "the collection sleeps where you do" means.

**Why.** The brief identifies the namesake framing as a major narrative asset — the hotel is the *anchor venue* of a gallery program named after it. That should be the single most distinctive thing about the section. The current manifesto buries it under MFA-thesis register.

**Fix.** Strip the first 1.5 sentences and let the concrete prose lead:

> *"Treehouse is the name of a gallery program. It is also the name of this house, because this house is where the program started. Drawings in the corridors. Ceramics on the courtyard ledge. Photography at the foot of the stairs. The collection rotates four times a year — and the work sleeps where you sleep. The rooms are not its backdrop. They are the room."*

Lose "curatorial idea", "vitrines", "moved that way". The reader is at an *adults-only botanical Michelin Key hotel*; they trust the curatorial seriousness without being told.

### 7. MAJOR — The Sanctuary's no-front-desk line is the strongest writing on the page and is under-used.

**What.** The second body paragraph in Sanctuary:

> *"There is no front desk. There is a person who already knows your flight, your coffee, and which courtyard chair gets the last of the sun."*

This is the line. This is the page's voice in two sentences. R1 noted it as the strongest line; it's still buried as paragraph 2 in section 2, in body-large text, between two other sections of running prose.

**Why.** The brief's voice anchor is "meaningful connection with locals" — that sentence *is* the meaningful connection. It contains: the operational specificity (no front desk), the personalization (your flight, your coffee), the named place (courtyard), the sensory image (last of the sun), and the wry "we've been watching" undertone that earns "adults-only" without saying it. It is the page's headline-quality sentence.

**Fix.** Two moves:

- Pull this paragraph out as the *Sanctuary pull quote* — bigger type, more air, treat it like the breath quote. Let it be the first thing the reader's eye lands on in the Sanctuary section, not the second paragraph.
- Echo its rhythm elsewhere on the page. The current pillar III ("Barrio — Santa Ana, the quietest plaza in Mérida, a slow walk from everywhere") could be retuned to match: *"Barrio — Santa Ana, the plaza that empties earliest, the cathedral two blocks away that you'll keep walking past."*

The line is the voice. Use it as the anchor and tune everything else toward it.

### 8. MAJOR — The Journal cards sound like the writer; the Curated cards sound like a concierge brochure.

**What.** Two cards side by side, same property, different sections:

Journal: *"A morning at Mercado Santiago. Where to stand. What to order. Why the cochinita stops being made by ten and how to be there before it does."*

Curated: *"The Honeymoon. Private breakfast in the courtyard. A cenote at dawn. Mezcal at the rooftop pool. A week you remember out of order."*

Journal: *"The dry season, slowly. February through April is when Santa Ana is most itself. A week of recommendations from the housekeeper who has lived on the next street for thirty years."*

Curated: *"In-Suite Wellness. Mayan-trained therapists at your door. Copal, cacao, cool stone. Nothing rushed, nothing leaving the room."*

The journal cards have *specific knowledge* and *named people* — cochinita stops being made by ten, housekeeper of thirty years. The curated cards have *bullet-style sensory triplets* — cenote-mezcal-rooftop, copal-cacao-stone. Both are tight. But "Mayan-trained therapists at your door" is the voice of a hotel; "the housekeeper who has lived on the next street for thirty years" is the voice of *this* hotel.

The Curated section is on the right track — *"A week you remember out of order"* is strong; *"Drive slowly. Return for siesta"* is strong; *"a driver who waits without checking the time"* is strong. But the openings of each card default to amenity language (Private breakfast, Mayan-trained therapists, A private driver).

**Why.** Journal got to be itself. Curated felt the gravitational pull of "we're selling packages now" and reverted. The discontinuity tells the reader: this section is the upsell.

**Fix.** Open each Curated card with a *moment*, not an amenity. Suggested rewrites:

- Honeymoon → *"Five mornings nobody else has access to. A cenote at dawn, mezcal at the rooftop, and a week you'll remember out of order."*
- Cenotes & Haciendas → *"Limestone water, then lunch on a working henequen estate. Our driver knows which gate to use. Return slowly. Sleep."* (Drops "By appointment" stiffness.)
- In-Suite Wellness → *"A door knock at the hour you chose. Copal, cacao, cool stone. The therapist trained in Mayan technique, the room kept dim, the bath drawn for after."*
- Private Airport Transfers → keep as is — *"A car waiting before you land. A driver who waits without checking the time"* is already the right voice.

### 9. MAJOR — CTAs default to generic verbs; the page's voice deserves better.

**What.** Every CTA on the page:
- *"Reserve your stay"* (hero primary)
- *"Walk under the leaves →"* (hero secondary) ← good
- *"Reserve this room →"* (rooms, ×3)
- *"Reserve this suite →"* (rooms)
- *"Reserve the penthouse →"* (rooms)
- *"Book a curator-led walkthrough →"* (art)
- *"Read entry →"* (journal, ×4)
- *"Open in maps →"* (location)
- *"Enquire"* (reserve form)
- *"Email Reservations"* (footer)
- Promo code: *"CANOPY30"*

*"Walk under the leaves"* is the one CTA that sounds like Tree House. Everything else is the verb the action requires. *"Reserve your stay"* is what the box at the top of every hotel website says. The Penthouse CTA "Reserve the penthouse" is closest in spirit because it specifies what's being reserved, but it's still doing the work of a verb, not a moment.

**Why.** The hero promised a writer. CTAs are the moments the reader is making a decision — they should sound like the writer is still there. *"Walk under the leaves"* works because it's spatial, sensory, low-pressure. The other CTAs do nothing the voice can't do.

**Fix.** Pick a CTA voice and commit. Options (suggestion: more like the secondary, less like the primary):

- Hero primary: *"Reserve your stay"* → *"Hold a key →"* or *"Hold a room under the canopy →"*
- Room CTAs: *"Reserve this room →"* → *"Hold this room →"* (consistent across the four; the room name does the specificity work)
- Art CTA: *"Book a curator-led walkthrough →"* → *"Walk the rotation with us →"*
- Journal: *"Read entry →"* → *"Read on →"* (smaller; the title carries)
- Form: *"Enquire"* → *"Write to us →"* or *"Ask the house →"*
- Location: *"Open in maps →"* → *"Find Calle 43 →"*

One verb family ("hold", "walk", "write", "find") — every CTA picks from it. *"Reserve"* belongs to the airline category; let Tree House have its own.

### 10. MAJOR — The Sanctuary section declares positioning instead of telling the property's story.

**What.** The Sanctuary headline + body:

> *"A house wrapped in leaves, kept quiet on purpose. Fifteen rooms. One canopy. A colonial house in Santa Ana, smothered in the kind of greenery that cools the air ten degrees before you've finished breathing in. Adults only — because some refuges are not for everyone, and ours is for the ones who want to be alone together."*

And the three pillars:
- I. Canopy: *"A microclimate of leaves. Ten degrees cooler than the street outside the gate."*
- II. Adults Only: *"Fifteen keys. No children. No lobby music. The mornings start when you do."*
- III. Barrio: *"Santa Ana — the quietest plaza in Mérida, a slow walk from everywhere."*

These are *good positioning lines*. They're not story. What the reader doesn't learn:
- How old is the canopy? (Decades-old trees? Planted when?)
- What kind of greenery? (Bougainvillea, palms, ceiba?)
- Whose colonial house was it? (Built when, by whom, restored when?)
- What does "ten degrees cooler" mean in Yucatán terms? (Twenty-eight to eighteen? Thirty-two to twenty-two?)
- Why Santa Ana and not Centro? (The trade-off?)

The brief's question 4 asks whether the Sanctuary tells a story *about this property* — right now it tells a story *about its positioning*.

**Why.** Largo's restraint is restraint *of detail you have*. Tree House's restraint reads as the absence of detail. The reader who is choosing between this hotel and three others in Mérida needs one or two specifics that *only this house* could provide. The canopy needs a number (how many trees, how old, what species). The barrio needs a temperature differential the reader could verify by stepping outside. The colonial house needs an age.

**Fix.** Add 2–3 *specific* trust-signal sentences to the Sanctuary body — not as bullets, as the writer's voice. Example insertion:

> *"The colonial house was built in 1898 and quietly opened, after a four-year restoration, in 2019. The trees that wrap it are older. Three ceibas, two flamboyanes, the rest a botanical accumulation of fifty years. The microclimate is real — 32°C on Calle 43 at three in the afternoon; 22°C under the courtyard at the same hour."*

Numbers that can be checked. Species that can be photographed. A restoration year that anchors the prose. (Substitute real values; the brief calls these out as "trust signals".)

### 11. MINOR — The breath quote works; the interstitial quote is weaker.

**What.** Two pull quotes, mid-page:

Breath section: *"Some houses keep you from the heat. This one keeps you from the noise."* — Field notes, March

Interstitial: *"Una casa que respira hojas."* — A house that breathes leaves.

The breath quote is the page's best pull quote — it makes a *distinction* (heat vs noise) that other hotels can't make. It earns its place. The interstitial is poetic but vapid — "a house that breathes leaves" is what an AI would write if asked for a botanical hotel tagline. It's pretty; it says nothing.

**Why.** Pull quotes are the page's two seconds of full attention. The breath quote uses those two seconds to position the property (we are not selling you AC; we are selling you silence). The interstitial uses them to be decorative.

**Fix.** Replace the interstitial with something with stakes. Suggested:

> *"En la casa, el silencio tiene horario."* — In this house, silence keeps a schedule.

Or:

> *"Quince llaves. Ninguna prisa."* — Fifteen keys. No hurry.

Or pull from the no-front-desk line:

> *"No hay recepción. Hay alguien que ya sabe en qué silla cae la última luz."* — No front desk. There is someone who already knows which chair the last light falls on.

Pick one that *says something* — heat/noise distinction is the model.

### 12. MINOR — Headline italic-noun discipline is consistent now (R1 fix), but two H2 italics still read as ornament.

**What.** R1 stripped italic from grammatical tails ("to disappear", "a little longer", "Journal.", "Mérida.") — correct call. What remains:

- Sanctuary: *"wrapped in **leaves**"* — meaningful (the noun the property is about)
- Michelin: *"the first **Michelin Key**"* — meaningful (the prestige claim)
- Location: *"A house on **Calle 43**"* — meaningful (the specific address)
- Offers: *"30% off"* — italic on a percentage; pulls the eye but isn't the noun
- Hero: *"Tree **House**"* + *"**Fifteen rooms. One canopy.**"* — the hero's two deliberate beats

Two remaining italics earn it less clearly. *"30% off"* in italic is just emphasis on a number; the percentage doesn't need typographic help — it'll get attention anyway. The hero tagline's full-italic *"Fifteen rooms. One canopy."* is fine as a one-off but pre-echoes the same phrase in the Sanctuary opening (which is *not* italic) — the reader gets the same line in two type treatments within 1.5 screens. The repetition becomes the noticed thing, not the meaning.

**Why.** R1's italic-discipline pass left this clean; these are the two surviving items to consider. Minor — the page is already tighter than at R1 — but if R2 is the voice pass, voice-meaning of italic is part of it.

**Fix.**

- *"30% off"* — drop italic; let the number speak.
- Hero tagline — either (a) drop italic from the tagline so the H1 *"Tree House"* is the lone italic moment in the hero, or (b) drop the "Fifteen rooms. One canopy." opener from the Sanctuary body (it's redundant with the tagline that sits 200px above it). Probably (b): the Sanctuary should start with "*A colonial house in Santa Ana, smothered in the kind of greenery…*" — the hero already established the fifteen/one count.

### 13. MINOR — Adults-only voice is implied, not earned; one or two sharper lines would do it.

**What.** "Adults only" appears as: a hero eyebrow, a hero meta item ("18+"), a Sanctuary headline kicker, a pillar title (Adults Only · "Fifteen keys. No children. No lobby music. The mornings start when you do."), and a footer line ("Sólo adultos"). The voice is correct — wry, contemplative, never breathy. *"The mornings start when you do"* is the right register.

What's missing: the *grown-up* dimension. The brief specifies "slightly grown-up, slightly wry, contemplative". The current copy delivers "quiet" but rarely delivers "grown-up" — there's no line that nods to the *kind* of adult who books this place. The "ones who want to be alone together" line in Sanctuary gestures at it; the "cicadas exactly as loud as a room of two people should be" testimonial nails it; the Penthouse description gestures at it ("the whole Mérida sky between you and anyone who isn't here" — in my proposed Finding #3 rewrite).

**Why.** "Adults only" without an adult voice is just "no children". The category — adults-only luxury — is full of properties that sell *exclusion*. Tree House should sell *the kind of company you keep when you're here*. One or two lines that specifically address the couple-on-anniversary, the writer-on-deadline, the friend-pair-on-pilgrimage tonality would distinguish from "quiet hotel".

**Fix.** Add one line, somewhere in Sanctuary or Curated, that names what the adults are doing. Suggestions:

- *"For couples on their second decade and friends on their fortieth year. For people who have finished apologizing for sleeping in."*
- *"Fifteen rooms. No weddings. No groups. No reason to be downstairs by nine."*
- *"The bar pours until last guest. The pool is open at five in the morning. The mornings, again, start when you do."*

Pick one. The voice is there; it just needs to point at the audience once.

## What's working (coda)

The strongest writing on the page is genuinely strong:

- **Hero tagline** *"Where the courtyard breathes. Fifteen rooms. One canopy."* — three beats, sensory, restrained, distinct. This is the voice anchor.
- **No-front-desk paragraph** — the page's best sentence. Surface it (Finding #7).
- **Pillar III, Sanctuary** *"Fifteen keys. No children. No lobby music. The mornings start when you do."* — the adults-only voice working at full strength.
- **Breath quote** *"Some houses keep you from the heat. This one keeps you from the noise."* — the kind of distinction that earns a pull quote.
- **Journal section** — entirely on voice. *"Why the cochinita stops being made by ten and how to be there before it does"* is the single most credible line on the page (you can hear someone who has actually missed the cochinita).
- **Hero secondary CTA** *"Walk under the leaves →"* — the one CTA written in the right voice.
- **Penthouse Canopy line** *"The top of the tree"* — the room name and its first beat are perfect.
- **Field notes attribution** on the breath quote — frames the source as the writer's own notebook, which is the right framing for a single-writer voice site.
- **R1 Spanish fix** — *"Notas escritas al partir"* is idiomatic and elegant; well chosen.
- **Trust-signal locations** are present (Calle 43 × 58 y 60, #489; 40 metres to Plaza Santa Ana; MID 15 min) — the data is there. The voice needs to do the same elsewhere (Finding #10).

The voice exists on this page; it just isn't yet the only voice on this page. R2's work is mostly *editing out the other voices* — Michelin press release, art-fair statement, concierge brochure — and letting the writer who wrote the hero, the no-front-desk line, and the journal carry the rest.
