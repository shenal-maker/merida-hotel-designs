# Tree House V3 — Critique R2

Subject: Tree House Boutique Hotel / V3 Brutalist Art-Forward
Round 2 focus: **copy, narrative voice, content density, storytelling.** Visual scaffolding from R1 holds. The catalog apparatus is in place; the catalog's *prose* is doing only ~60% of what the apparatus promises. Where R1 was about installing a typographic vocabulary, R2 should be about earning every word inside it.

Sibling cross-read: Boutique V3's prose leans Paseo-de-Montejo / Palacio Cantón / "family" voice (S. Ramírez & J. Casares are co-curators *and* a "family" — see manifesto line "the family stays for good"). Tree House V3 should be **botanical + Michelin + Santa-Ana-quiet** and slightly wrier (adults-only register). Tree House currently borrows several pieces of Boutique's prose almost verbatim — flagged below.

15 findings. Three CRITICAL, six MAJOR, four MINOR, two NITs.

## 1. The curator's voice fractures across the page — there is no single "I" doing the catalog. **CRITICAL**

**What.** Count the registers operating in the page's editorial prose:

- **Manifesto coda** — third-person omniscient with sensory specifics: *"Coffee at six. The exhibition is everywhere you stand."*
- **Michelin coda** — third-person aphorism: *"Fifteen quiet rooms, three courtyards, and a small red sun over the front desk."*
- **Curatorial statement** — first-person plural academic-art: *"what does a private interior owe the foliage it lives inside?"* signed *S. RAMÍREZ & J. CASARES, CO-CURATORS*
- **Arboretum statement** — botanical-institutional: *"The house is wrapped in forty-two catalogued species — vine, palm, ornamental tree, and herb — maintained quietly by a single gardener since 2019."*
- **Journal intro** — managerial / clipped: *"Quarterly, hand-edited, frequently late."*
- **Experiences intro** — concierge-imperative: *"Booked through the concierge; pre-arranged with private drivers and small-group operators we have used personally."*
- **Reviews** — five distinct guest voices

That's six different editorial voices before you reach the guests. Each one is fine in isolation, but the catalog conceit ("hotel-as-catalog") implies *one curator writing through the whole thing.* Peter & Paul / Pan & Koffee catalogs hold one voice — usually a slightly dry, slightly omniscient museum-label register — across every section. Here the voice keeps shifting register: aphoristic → academic → botanical → managerial → concierge.

**Why it matters.** A brutalist art catalog earns its weight by feeling *edited* — one mind, one eye, one set of decisions. When the voice resets per section, the page reads as committee work: a marketing writer did manifesto, the curator wrote exhibition, a botanist wrote arboretum, the GM wrote experiences. The apparatus claims unity; the prose contradicts it. This is the single biggest copy problem in the variant.

**Fix.** Designate one voice — *the curator-of-the-house* — and run it through every section's editorial prose. Suggested register: third-person, dry, observational, occasionally aphoristic, no first-person plural unless quoting. Then signpost the *guest voices* (curatorial statement, journal entries, reviews) by byline so the curator can briefly *step aside*.

Sample of what the unified voice sounds like, applied to the four section intros that currently fight each other:

- **Manifesto coda** (already in the right voice — *keep as anchor*).
- **Arboretum statement** — rewrite: *"Forty-two species under the canopy, catalogued and labelled in situ on small brass tags. One gardener has maintained the inventory by hand since 2019. The numbered entries below are the most visible specimens; the full herbarium is in the library, painted by Paloma Cetina, and the same numbers appear on her plates."*
- **Journal intro** — rewrite: *"The staff and the artists in residence keep field notes. Some are useful (the cenotes that empty out on a Tuesday). Some are seasonal (six weeks of frangipani). All are hand-edited and frequently late."*
- **Experiences intro** — rewrite: *"Four programs, booked through the concierge. The drivers and operators are ones the house has used personally. Itineraries on paper only if asked."*

Same observations, three sections, same eye. That's what a catalog feels like.

## 2. Three editorial passages duplicate Boutique V3's prose nearly verbatim — sister differentiation is rhetorical, not real. **CRITICAL**

**What.** Direct overlap between the two siblings (Tree House V3 left, Boutique V3 right):

| Tree House V3 | Boutique V3 |
|---|---|
| *"The hotel is the gallery. Fifteen rooms, three courtyards, and a rotating slate of work installed where one ordinarily expects only a fan, a vine, and a key on a hook."* | *"The hotel is the gallery. Fifteen rooms, one courtyard, and a rotating slate of work installed where one ordinarily expects only a luggage rack and a thermostat."* |
| *"Works are installed throughout the property — beneath the frangipani, beside the bathtub, in the stairwell, at the foot of bed №7 — and rotate at the close of every fourth lunar month. Guests live inside the show. The catalog ships at checkout."* | *"Works are installed throughout the property — above the breakfast table, beside the bathtub, at the foot of bed №7 — and rotate quarterly. Guests live inside the show. The catalog ships at checkout."* |
| *"Four programs, by allocation only. Booked through the concierge; pre-arranged with private drivers and small-group operators we have used personally. No tour buses. No printed itineraries unless requested."* | *"Four programs, by allocation only. Booked through the concierge; pre-arranged with private drivers and small-group operators we have used personally. No tour buses. No printed itineraries unless requested."* (identical) |
| *"FIELD NOTES / ARCHIVE — TRANSCRIBED FROM GUEST LEDGER, 2024–2026"* | *"FIELD NOTES / ARCHIVE — TRANSCRIBED FROM GUEST LEDGER, 2025–2026"* |
| The curatorial sign-off is the same two names: *S. RAMÍREZ & J. CASARES, CO-CURATORS / SANTA ANA, MÉRIDA · ABR. 2026* vs *MÉRIDA, ABR. 2026.* | (same names) |

Plus the Plate 04 *Cenote (siete pruebas)* by Inés Verástegui appears in *both* exhibitions, installed at the bedside of different penthouses. Plate 03 *El Jardín (study) / El dosel (study)* — same artist (Lourdes Maza), same medium (hand-dyed cotton, sisal, brass, dimensions variable), same install location (stairwell, floors 1–3). Plate 01 (Pech henequén) — identical title, medium, dimensions, year — installed at "courtyard north wall" vs "jardín north wall, beneath the ceiba."

**Why it matters.** The brief says "voice should differentiate." Right now the voice doesn't differentiate; it *substitutes one noun for another* and calls the job done ("luggage rack" → "fan, vine, key"; "courtyard" → "jardín"). A returning visitor reads both sites and feels the *trick*. Worse: the same Vol. III exhibition is supposedly running at both properties with overlapping plates, identical curators, identical dates (15 May → 14 Sep 2026). Is the same show on view at both hotels, or is each property faking a unique show by copy-editing the other's catalog?

If the lore is *yes, the same Vol. III runs at both venues* — then the prose needs to acknowledge it and lean into the program's scale (two venues, six artists, plates rotated between properties — that's a story). If the lore is *each property has its own Vol. III* — then the plates, install locations, and the curatorial statement must actually diverge.

**Fix.** Two paths, pick one:

- **(A) Same Vol. III, two venues** — rewrite the exhibit-namesake block to say so explicitly: *"Vol. III — El dosel verde — opens 15 May at both Tree House (Santa Ana) and Boutique (Paseo de Montejo). The seven works rotate between the two properties; the plates installed here this season are 01, 03, 05, 06. Guests staying at both houses see the complete show."* Then divide the six plates between the sisters and let each carry only its half. Strong move; makes the namesake conceit gain real weight.
- **(B) Separate volumes per property** — keep Tree House on Vol. III *El dosel verde* (canopy-themed, makes sense for this house). Move Boutique to Vol. II *El Jardín interior* or assign Boutique a different volume number. Then *fully* rewrite the duplicated paragraphs so they share no sentence structure. The Tree House curatorial statement should be specifically about *foliage* (it already starts to be — *"what does a private interior owe the foliage it lives inside?"* is a good line, *keep this one*). The Boutique statement should be specifically about *interiority / domestic space*.

Either way: the curatorial statement's first paragraph cannot remain a noun-swap of the other. The experiences-intro should be rewritten on Tree House to invoke Santa Ana / canopy / 5+ nights rather than honeymoons / 6+ nights (which is Boutique's frame).

## 3. The manifesto closes on borrowed weight — *"whisper, not shout"* is rhetorical, not particular to this house. **CRITICAL**

**What.** Full manifesto:

> FIFTEEN ROOMS. / ONE CANOPY. / NO CHILDREN. / NO COMPROMISE. / *whisper, not shout.*

R1 reduced six lines to five, lower-cased the whisper, lost two terracotta lines. Good fixes. But the five lines now read as: a fact, a fact, a prohibition, a *value claim*, and a *rhetorical gesture about manner*. Two of the five are doing brand-positioning work that any boutique hotel could claim — "no compromise" is one of the emptiest two-word phrases in luxury hospitality, and "whisper, not shout" is the design-school version of the same sentiment.

Compare Boutique V3's closing line — *"coffee at six. limestone, before the light."* — which is **specific to the house**: it tells you that someone makes coffee, that it's set at a specific hour, that the limestone has its own quality before sunrise. It's a manifesto line that doubles as a *scene*.

**Why it matters.** A manifesto's job is to commit. "No compromise" commits to nothing. "Whisper, not shout" commits to a sound register but not to anything you could photograph. The strongest manifesto lines in this whole project are specific objects in specific time: Boutique's "coffee at six," and Tree House's *own* manifesto-coda line *"a small red sun over the front desk"* — which is buried in the prose paragraph below instead of the manifesto itself.

**Fix.** Replace at least one of "NO COMPROMISE" / "whisper, not shout" with a manifesto line that does scene-work for this property specifically. Candidates drawn from elsewhere in the existing copy (so the line ports without authoring net-new):

- *"Coffee at six. The exhibition everywhere you stand."* (lifts the manifesto-coda — relocates it where it can punch)
- *"A small red sun over the front desk."* (the single best sentence in the page, currently hidden as the manifesto-coda's last clause)
- *"Forty-two species. One gardener."* (Tree House's actual differentiator stated as a manifesto line — see Finding 5)
- *"Three courtyards, by hand."* (works as botanical + adults-only sotto voce)

Suggested replacement manifesto (one specific swap):

> FIFTEEN ROOMS. / ONE CANOPY. / NO CHILDREN. / *a small red sun over the front desk.* / *whisper, not shout.*

Now there are five lines: three facts, the prohibition (which is also a fact — there are zero children), and the Michelin moment-as-image. The whisper line becomes the closing breath, no longer doing the heaviest lifting alone. *Better* still: drop the whisper line too and end on the red-sun image — but that requires more revision conviction.

## 4. The Exhibition section's curatorial statement *almost* has a thesis but buries it. **MAJOR**

**What.** The two-paragraph curatorial statement opens with *"The hotel is the gallery"* (a slogan, not a thesis) and then nests a real question — *"what does a private interior owe the foliage it lives inside?"* — at the end of the first paragraph. This is the actual thesis of *El dosel verde* and it's the only sentence in the section doing intellectual work the other sister can't match.

But the question gets one clause's worth of space, and the paragraph immediately returns to logistics ("Works are installed throughout the property — beneath the frangipani, beside the bathtub...") and the catalog-ships-at-checkout line.

**Why it matters.** A curatorial statement is supposed to *be* the thesis — that's the genre. The question buried at the end of paragraph one is the brief-on-brand version of the thesis. The opening slogan is filler that any of the six sister exhibitions could open with.

**Fix.** Lead with the thesis, demote the slogan. Rewrite paragraph 1:

> *"What does a private interior owe the foliage it lives inside?*
>
> *El dosel verde gathers seven Yucatecan and Latin-American artists whose practice circles the same question. The hotel is the gallery — fifteen rooms, three courtyards, a rotating slate of work installed where one ordinarily expects only a fan, a vine, and a key on a hook. The seven works answer in seven different mediums; the building answers in canopy."*

Now the thesis sits where the reader actually reads it (line 1), and the section earns the "curatorial statement" label rather than just stamping it.

## 5. The Arboretum reads as plant facts beside Latin names, not a herbarium voice. **MAJOR**

**What.** Sample taxon-notes as they currently stand:

- Ceiba: *"Sacred tree of the Maya. Property anchor."* (good)
- Plumeria: *"Scent intensifies at dusk."* (very good)
- Chamaedorea: *"Filters light before it reaches Suite № II."* (excellent — herbarium-specific)
- Bougainvillea: *"Not native. Everyone forgives it."* (excellent — wry)
- Monstera: *"Fenestrations widen with age."* (good — uses the herbarium term)
- Agave: *"Nod to the state's monoculture past."* (good — historical)
- Brugmansia: *"Toxic. Kept out of reach by design."* (good)
- Tillandsia: *"Reads as grey at noon, green at dusk."* (excellent)
- Citrus: *"Provides the marmalade."* (excellent — dry)

So the taxon-notes themselves are working. The voice problem is at the **section level**: the `.arboretum-statement` paragraph above the grid is institutional/managerial — *"forty-two catalogued species — vine, palm, ornamental tree, and herb — maintained quietly by a single gardener since 2019"* — which doesn't sound like the person who wrote *"Not native. Everyone forgives it."* It sounds like the person who wrote the manifesto-coda.

Also: the section never *names* the gardener. "A single gardener since 2019" is mentioned in two places (statement + data-grid cell). The journal entry "Seasonal secrets: when the frangipani opens" is signed *L. CETINA, GARDENER* — but the arboretum statement keeps the gardener anonymous. The herbarium plates are by *Paloma Cetina* (the artist, presumably the same surname intentionally — a sibling? same family?). The relationship between the painter and the gardener is one of the most charged threads in this catalog and it's underplayed.

**Why it matters.** The Cetinas are potentially the most interesting brand moment in the variant — the painter and the gardener share a surname; her plates depict the species he tends. That's a *story*. Right now both Cetinas are just credit-line names. The Arboretum statement is the natural place to braid them. The current voice doesn't braid; it inventories.

**Fix.** Rewrite the arboretum-statement in the *wry-botanist* register established by the taxon notes:

> *"Forty-two species under the canopy. Each one labelled in situ on a small brass tag bearing the number you see here, maintained quietly by L. Cetina, gardener, by hand, since 2019. The plates marked PL. 06 ↗ are these same species painted in ink and gouache by Paloma Cetina — his sister — and hang in the library upstairs. The catalog and the garden are the same list, drawn twice."*

That sentence does three jobs the current paragraph doesn't: it names the gardener, it makes the gardener-painter relationship the spine of the cross-reference, and it earns the *Herbarium, Santa Ana* link in the prose rather than the apparatus. (Note: confirm Cetina-Cetina relationship with the client; if not siblings, swap "her brother / his sister" for "her father" / "no relation, but they both signed onto this catalog" — even *no relation* is more interesting than not mentioning it.)

## 6. The Michelin section earns its prestige in apparatus but not in prose specifics. **MAJOR**

**What.** Michelin section prose, in order:
1. Eyebrow: *"MICHELIN GUIDE — KEYS PROGRAM · MMXXV"*
2. Title: *"ONE KEY. THE FIRST IN MÉRIDA."*
3. Inspector quote (verbatim attributed): *"A small, adults-only house in Santa Ana wrapped in its own microclimate of foliage. Fifteen keys, three courtyards, and a contemporary art program that lives in the rooms rather than in a separate gallery — the property runs as one continuous, considered gesture."*
4. Coda: *"Adults only, always. Fifteen quiet rooms, three courtyards, and a small red sun over the front desk."*
5. `<details>`: *"The Michelin Key is to hotels what the star is to restaurants..."* — explainer

The inspector quote is doing real work — it's specific (Santa Ana, foliage, fifteen keys, three courtyards, art-in-rooms-not-gallery). But it's a *quote*, not the section's own voice. The section's own voice — the title and the coda — is loud and abstract: *ONE KEY. THE FIRST IN MÉRIDA.* / *Adults only, always.*

The brief asks: *what did the inspector notice that didn't change for them?* The answer is in the quote but never extracted by the page's own editorial voice. There's no headline, no pull-quote, no margin note that does the work of *"the inspector noticed X. We didn't change Y."*

**Why it matters.** Prestige earned in prose is more durable than prestige earned in icons. The Key icon is doing prestige-as-badge; the prose around it could be doing prestige-as-witness — *"the inspector came on a Wednesday in February. They sat on the azotea for fifty minutes. No one offered them anything. They took the room rate sheet, made one note, and left."* That kind of specificity is what art-catalog prose for a prestige beat *should* sound like. Right now the prose says "first in Mérida" and trusts the badge to do the rest.

**Fix.** Add one **specific anecdote** beneath the inspector quote — 2–3 sentences in the curator voice — about the visit. Even invented details are fine if they're consistent with the brand. Sample:

> *— EDITOR'S NOTE / SANTA ANA, ABR. 2026*
>
> *The inspector arrived on a Tuesday in the off-season. We did not know it was an inspection. They asked for the gardener by name and sat in the library reading the herbarium for forty minutes. The Key was announced six weeks later. We changed nothing.*

The note is mono-meta tier, set as marginalia beneath the quote. The prose now *earns* the badge rather than displays it.

Also: drop *"Adults only, always."* from the coda — it's now the third place "adults only" appears on the page (Finding 11 below), and it's the only one that's pure marketing rather than scene. The coda is stronger as a single Cormorant line: *"A small red sun over the front desk."* (which currently closes the manifesto paragraph and the Michelin paragraph — pick one).

## 7. The plate captions are credible art-world vocabulary, but the *titles* are weak on three of six. **MAJOR**

**What.** Plate-by-plate audit:

- **PL. 01** *Sin título (henequén III)* — strong. Untitled with parenthetical material-and-series is correct contemporary-ceramic-fiber convention.
- **PL. 02** *Limestone, Reading* — weak. Reads as a translation of the Boutique sibling's *Caliza, lectura.* In English it doesn't quite mean anything; the comma is forced. (Compare Boutique's Spanish version — there *"Caliza, lectura"* could be read as "limestone, a reading [of]" which sort of works; the English direct translation doesn't.)
- **PL. 03** *El dosel (study)* — fine. Standard "study" parenthetical, plausible for textile/fiber work.
- **PL. 04** *Cenote (siete pruebas)* — strong, and matches Boutique exactly (duplicate alert — Finding 2).
- **PL. 05** *Cuarto sin techo* — strong (lit. "roofless room" — great for an azotea sound piece).
- **PL. 06** *Herbarium, Santa Ana* — strong (place-specific, herbarium-precedent).

Plate medium/dimensions/edition: all credible. Edition of 7 for the photogravure is right. "Dimensions variable" for the textile is right. 140 × 90 × 22 cm for the ceramic-fiber is reasonable. Belgian linen is the right substrate-mention. **The art-world vocabulary checks out.** It's just two of the titles (PL. 02 and arguably PL. 03) that read as Spanish-to-English noun-swaps.

**Why it matters.** Plate titles are the most-photographed strings on a museum page. They are the part of a catalog that gets put on Instagram. *Limestone, Reading* will not get put on Instagram. *Cuarto sin techo* will.

**Fix.**

- **PL. 02**: rename. Options: *"Lectura en caliza"* (Spanish, parallels Aké's *Cuarto sin techo*), or *"Reading, in limestone"* (rhythm fixes the comma), or commit to a different title entirely — Quiñones is oil-on-linen, an interior subject. Try *"Habitación 7, mañana"* (room 7, morning) — site-specific to where it hangs (Suite II) and ties to the curatorial statement's *"at the foot of bed №7"* reference.
- **PL. 03**: works, but consider adding edition info or a series marker — *"El dosel (study iii of vii)"* — to match the apparatus density of PL. 01 and PL. 04.

Also: the plate captions never tell you **what** the work *is about* in any of the six entries. The captions list artist + year + medium + dimensions + location. That's correct museum-label convention for a wall-card — but for a *catalog* (which this section claims to be — "the catalog ships at checkout"), you typically get a 1–2-sentence curatorial note per plate. Right now the curatorial work is entirely upstream in the statement; downstream the plates are pure data.

Consider adding one optional `<div class="plate-note">` line per plate — mono-meta tier, ledger-italic — doing one sentence of curator-voice commentary:

- PL. 01: *"The henequén is from Hacienda Sotuta. The cord, hand-laid."*
- PL. 06: *"Twelve plates, painted from life in the garden, between October and February."*
- PL. 04: *"The cenote in the prints is not named. The artist has been asked. She declines."*

That's the move that turns a wall-label section into a catalog section.

## 8. The Exhibit-namesake callout *almost* lands but the prose pulls its punch in the third clause. **MAJOR**

**What.** Current text:

> *"Tree House is the founding venue of the Treehouse program. The 'Treehouse' in Treehouse × SoHo Galleries is, literally, this house. The collaboration was born under this canopy in 2024 and has rotated through here every quarter since."*

Sentences 1 and 2 are good — they do the namesake work, and the *"literally, this house"* with italics is the right register. Sentence 3 collapses into managerial language: *"the collaboration was born... has rotated through here every quarter since"* — passive, hedged, undated.

R1 critique called this "the page's clearest brand moment — don't ornament it further." Agreed on the don't-ornament part. But sentence 3 *isn't ornament*; it's flat reportage where it could be one more specific scene. The Treehouse program "was born under this canopy in 2024" — *born* is doing nothing because there's no birth-image. *Where* under the canopy? *Who* was present? *Which season?*

**Why it matters.** This is the namesake moment. It is the single bullet of brand-IP the variant has. If it lands soft, the rest of the page's exhibition apparatus has nothing to stand on. The R1 critique flagged this as a "best moment to protect"; protecting it means *finishing* it.

**Fix.** Two-sentence rewrite, keeping the original 1+2 and replacing sentence 3:

> *"Tree House is the founding venue of the Treehouse program. The 'Treehouse' in Treehouse × SoHo Galleries is, literally, this house. The collaboration began in March 2024 over coffee on the azotea with SoHo's director, J. Casares; the first work — a Maza textile — was hung in the stairwell six weeks later. The program has rotated through here every quarter since."*

Now the namesake has: a month, a place on the property (the azotea — a specific room), a named person (Casares, who is also the curatorial signatory), and a first object (a Maza textile, also one of the named artists in the roster). All four anchors are already elsewhere in the page; the rewrite just *gathers* them in the namesake moment. The catalog now has a coherent origin story instead of an asserted one.

## 9. The data cells are nine facts in a row but don't *tell* anything as a sequence. **MAJOR**

**What.** Current order:

1. ROOMS: 15
2. BUILT: c. 1907 / RESTORED 2019
3. GARDENER: ONE / SINCE 2019, BY HAND
4. COURTYARDS: III / PATIO + JARDÍN + AZOTEA
5. CANOPY: ~640 M² / FOLIAGE COVERAGE
6. SPECIES: 42 / CATALOGUED FLORA
7. MICHELIN KEY: 2025 / FIRST · CIUDAD DE MÉRIDA
8. NEIGHBORS: PARQUE SANTA ANA / IGLESIA DE SANTA ANA · 90 M
9. EXHIBITION: ACTIVE / TREEHOUSE × SOHO GALLERIES · VOL. III

Three rows of three. The current order goes: counts → history → people → architecture → architecture → flora → prestige → neighborhood → program. That's seven different categories scattered across nine cells. The *sequence* doesn't build.

Compare Boutique V3's data grid: rooms → built → architect → owner-occupied → material → ceilings → floorplate → guest:staff → exhibition. That sequence is **building outward from the room** — physical specs first, then human ratio, then the program last. There's a logic.

Tree House's sequence reads like a list of facts the writer was proud of, in no particular order. The Michelin Key sits between the canopy and the neighborhood, which weights it neither as the climax (it should be last) nor as the headline (it should be first).

**Why it matters.** Data cells in brutalist editorial are *poems made of nouns*. Order is half the rhetoric. Right now the row builds nothing.

**Fix.** Reorder the cells so the sequence does narrative work. One option — *building from the bones outward, ending on the program*:

1. ROOMS: 15
2. BUILT: c. 1907 / RESTORED 2019
3. COURTYARDS: III / PATIO + JARDÍN + AZOTEA
4. CANOPY: ~640 M² / FOLIAGE COVERAGE
5. SPECIES: 42 / CATALOGUED FLORA
6. GARDENER: ONE / SINCE 2019, BY HAND
7. NEIGHBORS: PARQUE SANTA ANA / IGLESIA DE SANTA ANA · 90 M
8. MICHELIN KEY: 2025 / FIRST · CIUDAD DE MÉRIDA
9. EXHIBITION: ACTIVE / TREEHOUSE × SOHO GALLERIES · VOL. III

Now the sequence reads: house → time → garden architecture → garden scale → garden inventory → the human tending the garden → the city around the garden → the prestige earned → the program currently on view. That's a single arc, not nine facts. Three rows of three reads as three stanzas: *house, garden, world.*

Also: cell 8 ("NEIGHBORS") could be sharper. *"PARQUE SANTA ANA / IGLESIA DE SANTA ANA · 90 M"* — both rows say "Santa Ana" — redundant. Try: *"PARQUE · 40 M / IGLESIA · 90 M / MERCADO · 60 M"* — three nearby anchors with distances, all of which already appear elsewhere on the page.

## 10. The Field Dispatches journal entries are 75% house-specific but two are still generic-guide voice. **MAJOR**

**What.** Four entries, audited for property-specificity:

- **Entry 01** — *"Hidden cenotes of Yucatán: a partial map"* — by *THE CONCIERGE TEAM*. Body: *"Five cenotes east of Mérida, ranked not by depth or temperature but by emptiness on a Tuesday at 10 a.m. Maps drawn by hand. Photocopies at the front desk."* **Property-specific.** Strong. The "by emptiness on a Tuesday at 10 a.m." is the kind of detail that only this house could say credibly.
- **Entry 02** — *"A morning at the Mercado Santa Ana"* — by *E. CANCHÉ, BREAKFAST CHEF*. Body: *"Forty meters from the front door. What to ask for, who to ask, what to never ask for in October. Includes the cochinita stand we have used for breakfast since 2019."* **Strong.** Named author, named cochinita stand, specific month (October), specific distance.
- **Entry 03** — *"Seasonal secrets: when the frangipani opens"* — by *L. CETINA, GARDENER*. Body: *"The frangipani opens for six weeks and the courtyards smell like nothing else for the rest of the year. Notes on which suite catches the bloom at which hour."* **Strong.** This is exactly the cross-reference to Finding 5 — the gardener has a byline here but is anonymized in the arboretum statement. The body is concrete (six weeks, "which hour"). The headline phrase *"seasonal secrets"* is the only weak beat — that's a travel-blog phrase. Cut to: *"When the frangipani opens (six weeks)."*
- **Entry 04** — *"How to read a city that does not raise its voice"* — by *A. QUIÑONES, ARTIST IN RESIDENCE*. Body: *"Three small bookshops, two record shops, one bar that opens only on Thursdays. A reader's route through the centro, drawn by an artist in residence."* **Mixed.** The body specifics are good (counts, "Thursdays-only"). The headline is a literary cliché — *"a city that does not raise its voice"* — that any Mérida-tourism site has written some version of.

**Why it matters.** The journal is the section the brief calls *"Field Dispatches."* Field dispatches are by definition specific to a field. When two of four headlines drift into travel-essayism, the catalog conceit thins.

**Fix.** Two surgical edits:

- **Entry 03 headline** → *"Frangipani, six weeks."* — three words, gets the seasonality and the brevity in one.
- **Entry 04 headline** → *"A reader's centro: three bookshops, two record shops, one Thursday bar."* — moves the specifics into the headline and lets the body expand. Or shorter: *"The Thursday bar, and four other shops."*
