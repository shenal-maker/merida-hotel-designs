# Tree House Boutique — Photo Manifest

28 photos curated from the 119-photo shoot (89 Highlights + 30 Rooms). Total folder size ~8.5MB.

The property's signature subject is unmistakable in this shoot: a Mérida casona built around mature trees and a long linear water-channel that runs the spine of the courtyard, with a round white-stucco fountain at one end and waterfall spouts at the other. The photographer has captured this in *every light condition* — bright midday, soft afternoon, golden hour, twilight blue, and full night with property lighting. That breadth is the strongest material here.

## Role: Hero (2)

### `hero-tree-courtyard.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-27.jpg)*
**The hero.** A mature tree (looks like a higuera/strangler fig) literally growing through the courtyard with the two-story house wrapped around it. Dappled light, lanterns lit even at daytime, vines climbing balcony rails. This is the "tree house" in a single frame — no signage needed, no caption needed. Vertical orientation, works as full-bleed hero on any layout. Picked over alternates (28, 31) because the framing is the most generous: you see roots, trunk, canopy, the two balcony levels, and the courtyard floor in one composition.

### `hero-ficus-twilight.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-87.jpg)*
**Hero alt — evening register.** The giant root-buttressed ficus at blue hour, illuminated from below, with the linear pool lit cyan to the right and the waterfall spout running. Use this when the page wants drama instead of calm. Vertical. Chose over 81 and 82 because the *tree* is centered (which keeps "tree house" front of mind) rather than the fountain.

## Role: Sanctuary atmospheric (4)

### `sanctuary-arched-windows.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-11.jpg)*
Pair of tall arched steel-frame windows opening from the green-tile lounge onto the white-stucco fountain. Mid-century chairs in the foreground, palm fronds framing the courtyard. This is the "room with a view of the property" shot — interiority and exteriority in the same frame. Vertical.

### `sanctuary-staircase.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-29.jpg)*
Dark, moody — wooden ceiling slats, iron-and-wood staircase, two wire chairs in the foreground, a small tree-lit niche under the stairs. This is the quietest, most "adults-only" frame in the set. Vertical.

### `sanctuary-corridor-day.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-70.jpg)*
Upper-floor open-air corridor, daytime: wooden doors on the left, iron-rail arches opening to courtyard greenery on the right, ornate hanging lanterns. Vertical.

### `sanctuary-corridor-evening.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-80.jpg)*
Same corridor type but ground-floor and lit at night — palms spilling in from the left, glowing pendant lanterns, tile inlays on the polished concrete. Pairs with the day version for a before/after-style diptych if a variant wants one. Vertical.

## Role: Canopy / Botanical (4)

### `canopy-waterfall.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-25.jpg)*
The water-channel section: trees growing *out of* the pool, twin waterfall spouts down a tall plaster wall, fern fronds in the foreground. Vertical. The most "lush jungle hotel" image in the set. Chose over 35 (same area, less water motion) and 49 (cropped tighter).

### `canopy-tree-pool.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-50.jpg)*
Similar location but a different angle — the trees rising from the linear pool, water spout to the right, balcony with hanging lanterns in the background. Dappled, golden, intimate. Vertical.

### `canopy-courtyard-wing.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-54.jpg)*
**Horizontal establishing shot.** Wide view of an entire courtyard wing with the giant ficus trunk and aerial roots on the left, the long linear pool with waterfall on the right, multi-story stucco facades. Use this for full-width hero strips or banner contexts where vertical heroes don't fit.

### `canopy-overhead-pool.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-111.jpg)*
Overhead view of a plunge pool surrounded by lush palms and tropical foliage (calatheas, ferns). Wide horizontal. This is the closest the shoot gets to a "drone" feel even though it's shot from a balcony — pure canopy identity. Note: the DJI_* files in the Rooms folder are NOT drone shots; they're wide-angle interior room shots.

## Role: Rooms — four visually distinct (4)

### `room-limestone-skylight.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-95.jpg)*
**Limestone Skylight Room.** Wide horizontal showing the full room from a doorway: raw cut-stone limestone feature wall, three rectangular skylights cutting through the wall above the bed, walnut bed frame, geometric green-and-red cement floor tile. Distinct identity: light-from-above, rough material. Picked over 96, 97 (same room, tighter crops) because the doorway framing reads cleanly as "room hero."

### `room-balcony-doors.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-98.jpg)*
**Tree-Balcony Room.** A wholly different room — French doors flung open onto a wrought-iron balcony with a tree right outside, slat wood headboard, work desk with chair, TV. The "wake up to a tree in your face" room. Horizontal.

### `room-staggered-stick.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-107.jpg)*
**Staggered Stick Room.** Wide view of room with a striking pipe-organ-like vertical wood-stick headboard rising above the bed, two onyx wall sconces flanking it, geometric floor tile. The most sculptural/design-forward room. Distinct from the others by color (warm/airy) and by the headboard architecture. Chose over 105 (same room, vertical/closer) and 109 (close-up of headboard only — that one becomes a strip option, but is redundant if we already have 107).

### `room-slat-headboard.jpg` *(original: DJI_0194.jpg)*
**Slat Headboard Room.** Wide-angle DJI shot showing a fourth distinct room: tall slat wood headboard, chair-and-side-table corner, decorative-tile floor strip. Different from -107 because the headboard is solid-slat (not staggered) and the geometry is square not tapered. Horizontal.

> Note: 30 photos in the Rooms folder yielded effectively 4 distinct rooms repeated from multiple angles. If the property has more than 4 room types, retake-worthy gap.

## Role: Art / Curated vignette (3)

### `art-bar-figurines.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-18.jpg)*
Bar wall with grid-shelving, aged plaster behind it, walnut frames, and two bronze figurine sculptures perched on the shelves — actual art objects, not staging. Edison-bulb-and-rope chandelier overhead. This is the most explicit "art-in-place" image in the set. Horizontal-ish.

### `art-chair-tableau.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-43.jpg)*
Two mid-century chairs facing each other across a walnut table with a small eucalyptus posy, sitting on a layered patchwork of cement tiles. A wide raw-wood bench in the background holds a Panama hat and stacked books. This is the most magazine-cover composition the shoot has — gallery-feeling, intentional, no people. Horizontal.

### `art-window-painting.jpg` *(original: TreeHouse-93.jpg)*
Evening view from the courtyard looking through an arched steel-frame window into the lit lounge — you can see a colorful painting on the green wall inside, the rope chandelier, the chairs. Tree branches reach into the upper frame. This says "the hotel is a gallery you sleep in" without overstating it. Vertical.

## Photographer-strength notes

**What's strong:**
- The property captured at multiple times of day — full daylight, soft afternoon, twilight, and night-lit. That gives the website real range.
- Texture/material close-ups (limestone, tile, plaster, leaves, wood) are unusually good for a hotel shoot.
- The courtyard is shot from many angles and elevations — eye level, balcony level, looking back through windows. Lots of editorial possibilities.

**What's thin / potential gaps for re-shoot:**
- **Rooftop or exterior facade.** Almost nothing of the hotel from the street. If "Mérida casona" is part of the pitch, the entry/facade is missing.
- **Dining / restaurant.** Lots of bar shots and one dining-room shot (TreeHouse-16). No food, no plated dishes, no wine being poured. If F&B matters to the marketing, this is a gap.
- **Real guest moments.** The string quartet is the only "people" content. No couple-having-coffee, no someone-reading-by-the-pool. The current shoot is property-as-object rather than property-as-experience.
- **More than 4 room types?** The 30-photo Rooms folder repeats 4 rooms from multiple angles. If the property has additional categories (suites, junior suites, garden rooms), they aren't here.
- **The "DJI_" filenames are misleading.** They are NOT drone shots — they're wide-angle interior room photos shot with a DJI camera/gimbal. There appear to be no actual overhead drone shots of the courtyard. If aerial is wanted, that's an addition.

**Photos I was torn between:**
- Hero: TreeHouse-27 vs TreeHouse-50. -27 won because the tree-as-protagonist is more legible at a glance. -50 (canopy-tree-pool) keeps the water-channel identity and survives as a secondary canopy shot.
- Bar art: TreeHouse-18 vs TreeHouse-19/20/84. -18 has the cleanest composition with both bronze figurines visible and the bar itself in frame.
- The eucalyptus / tile shot was *almost* the third "art" pick — but it works harder as an editorial moment than as an art-collab section, so it went to overflow.
- Skipped entirely: the dining-room photo (TreeHouse-16) is good but isolated — without other F&B imagery it would feel orphaned on the site.
