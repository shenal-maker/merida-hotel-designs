01 / Nota de la Casa

Del cuaderno de The Editor's
Note

11 abril, MMXXVI. The ficus dropped a branch in the night — the first time in three rainy seasons it has asked for our attention. The gardener was out before the desk had finished the morning coffee. The house, on the other hand, has not asked for anything in seventy years. It was here before we were. We mostly try to keep up with it.

What we keep, then, is fifteen rooms in the barrio of Santa Ana — quince habitaciones, a courtyard, a small pool, and a rooftop the size of a long breakfast. Sin niños, by house rule, so that the canopy may keep its hush. The two ceibas at the corner of Calle 43 belong to the street; the neighbourhood will not let us prune them, and we have stopped suggesting it.

In MMXXV the Michelin Guide gave us a Key — the first awarded in Mérida. The news arrived by email at 9:14 a.m. on a Tuesday; the front desk was on a coffee break. (See Lámina II, opposite, for the corner of the patio at which the laptop was open.) We have not changed the towels since.

What you are holding is a quiet field journal of the house — rooms, plants, neighbours, art — bound in one volume. Pásela despacio.

— Apuntes del cuaderno verde Edición Botánica No. 1 · Santa Ana, Mérida, abril MMXXVI

02 / El Santuario
A House of Quince habitaciones — solo adultos
Lám. III — Ala del patio, dosel y agua

Un refugio botánico. A sanctuary cooled by its own canopy — in Santa Ana, two blocks from the park and one street back from the tour-bus route.

The building is colonial — one of the long low houses of Calle 43 — but the character of the house is the garden it grew into. Tree house, then, is literal: the walls are inside the leaves. At three in the afternoon (see Lám. XV) the courtyard runs noticeably cooler than the street.

The house keeps fifteen rooms, a single check-in, a small pool the colour of cenote-water, and a roof terrace shaded by a pair of grown Cocos nucifera. No children — política de la casa — and no parties louder than a long dinner.

What we kept: the original cantera floors, the cedar shutters, the iron-grille gates. What we added: linen, a curated front desk, a private gallery space (see Lám. X), and the kind of servicio discreto that knows your coffee by the second morning. The pool was added in 2021; the colour was chosen by a guest from Tulum who held a sample tile against a cenote photograph for forty minutes before she would agree.

03 / La Llave
One Michelin Key · MMXXV

Sobre La Llave.

In May 2025 the Michelin Guide named Tree House the first hotel in Mérida to receive a Key — the guide's hotel equivalent of the star. Una llave, then, in a city of restored colonial houses and not many small-hotel accolades.

The inspectors are silent about what tipped them; the Guide does not publish a written notice. What we can say is what we did not change for the inspection: the courtyard, the gardener, the fifteen rooms, the silence after ten. The cantera floors that wave a half-centimetre off level by the western shutter. The hour the canopy cools the patio (3:00 to 5:00, give or take, per the obsessive guest below). We took it that the building, the program and the staff were being recognised together — not service layered on a structure, but a house that had grown its own.

We do not write about it more than this. El que la tiene, la usa con cuidado.

05 / El Estándar

El Estándar

Three principles, kept in the order the canopy asks for them.

01
Privacidad

Privacy

Fifteen rooms. Solo adultos. No lobby loiter, no day-trippers through the gate, one front desk — never quite closed. Names remembered by the second morning; voices kept low. The canopy does the rest.

15 habitaciones · adults only
02
Botánica

Botany

Ficus benjamina (c. 1955, patio, ≈14 m at the canopy), Bougainvillea spectabilis (south wall, three vines grafted onto two trunks), Plumeria rubra, Cocos nucifera (rooftop, paired), and twenty-three potted Tillandsias the gardener refuses to name aloud — brought, the green cuaderno records, in a coffee tin from Campeche, 2019. We log every species. We do not log the gardener.

23 especies · al último recuento
03
Curaduría

Curation

The cenote a guest swims at, the mezcal poured on the rooftop, the canvas hung along the eastern corridor — each is chosen by someone who has driven the road, tasted the bottle, watched the painter work. The spring rotation through the corridors is signed by Magdalena Ríos; the henequen press at the hacienda we send guests to has been running since 1894. Nothing on this list arrived by catalogue.

06 / La Galería Bajo el Dosel
Este es el origen del nombre.

The Treehouse × SoHo Galleries

Every spring a new rotation arrives. SoHo Galleries Mérida curates the cycle; we hang the work through the corridors, the suites, the courtyard (see Láms. X–XIII) — el nombre vive aquí. Guests live inside the exhibition for the length of their stay.

“The work here lives in a humidity the gallery district does not have. Three of the canvases were stretched specifically for the Bougainvillea wing — the bougainvillea is not a metaphor; it is the room next door. Light falls on the work the way the leaves let it: some hours one canvas is lit, some hours another.”

El Mercado, antes que abra la cocina

Field notes · 6:20 a.m.

Most mornings our cook, Doña Marisol, walks to the Mercado de Santa Ana before the kitchen lights are on. Two stalls for fruit (the second is run by Mari, daughter of the first Mari), one for cochinita. The coffee on the corner opens at 6:30. Botánica de la Casa — un índice

Field notes · jardín

An index of every species in the courtyard: Ficus benjamina, Bougainvillea spectabilis, Plumeria rubra, Cocos nucifera, and the twenty-three Tillandsias the gardener will not let us name. Cuaderno — Nº 04

El jardinero, que no se llama Jardinero

Field notes · retrato

His name is Don Eulogio. He has worked on this courtyard since 1998 — longer than the current building has been a hotel, longer than the manager, longer than the espresso machine. He arrives at 5:40 a.m. He leaves when the patio cools. He will tell you about the ficus; he will not tell you about the Tillandsias. Erratum: Cuaderno No. 02 originally credited the cochinita stall to Doña Mari; her daughter, Mari, runs it now. — La Editora, abril MMXXVI.

Curated Mérida

Experiences arranged from the front desk — solo lo que recomendaríamos a un amigo.

  1. i.

    Luna de Miel — Bajo el Dosel

    Honeymoon package

    Champagne on the rooftop at sundown, a private dinner under the ficus, a sunrise breakfast in the canopy, a half-day at a cenote with no one else in it. Four nights minimum.

  2. ii.

    Cenotes & Haciendas

    Un día fuera de la ciudad

    A driver, a guide, a basket lunch. Three cenotes the tour buses do not know — one in Lám. XX, one closed to the public on Tuesdays, one reachable only by a friend of the house. Best before 10 a.m. One restored hacienda that still keeps its henequen press. Returned to the canopy by sundown.

    In-Suite Wellness

    Bienestar

    Massage in the room, a temazcal arranged with a local curandera, a slow yoga hour on the rooftop before the city is fully awake. Botanical teas drawn from the garden — chamomile, lemon-verbena, flor de Jamaica.
