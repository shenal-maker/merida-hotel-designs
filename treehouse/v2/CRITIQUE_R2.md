### 1. CRITICAL — The Editor's Note is structurally *the same paragraph* as Boutique V2's Editor's Note. Different nouns, identical move.

**What.** Tree House opens:
> "We did not plant the trees. They were here before we were — a *ficus* in the patio older than any of us…"  (`index.html:121`)

Boutique V2 opens (`/v2/index.html:115`):
> "We did not set out to build a hotel. We set out to keep a house — a long colonial home on Calle 43, with its high ceilings and inner courtyard…"

Same opener formula: **"We did not [verb the obvious]. [Inheritance / what was already there] — [list of three architectural/botanical features] — [thing we did not let disappear / let us prune]."** Both then progress to a "fifteen rooms" sentence with parenthetical Spanish. Both close with a signature. Both sit on a `drop-cap` paragraph.

Beyond structure, **specific tics repeat across siblings**:
- Both end with the same kind of cadence: "*Pásela despacio*" (Tree House) and the equivalent register in Boutique.
- Both use the construction **"X has been here longer than Y"** (Tree House: "the gardener has been here longer than the manager" `:381`; Boutique uses an equivalent ledger-against-staff line).
- Both rely on "We did not [X]. We did [Y]." reversal as a central rhetorical move.

**Why it matters.** The brief was *editorial voice consistency across siblings, but with a distinct field-journal register*. R1's fix focused on plate-reference numerics and drop-cap math but left the Editor's Note voice essentially a syntactic clone of Boutique's. A discerning reader landing on both pages — and that *is* the target reader (returning Mérida traveller, design-conscious) — will register the duplication immediately. The field journal has to sound *like a different writer's notebook* than the magazine, not like the same writer with a different vocabulary list.

**Fix.** Tree House's Editor's Note should not open with "We did not." That move belongs to Boutique now (it was published first in the sibling sequence). The Tree House voice should be a **botanist's logbook**, not a *patron's letter*. Try opening with a dated entry — concrete, observational, slightly clinical, then warming:

> *April 11, MMXXVI — The ficus dropped a branch in the night. It was the first time in three rainy seasons it had asked for our attention; the gardener was out before the desk had finished the morning coffee. The house, on the other hand, has not asked for anything in seventy years. It was here before we were. We mostly try to keep up with it.*

Different opener move (observational, dated, slightly wry). Different cadence. Same Spanish-English bilingualism allowed, but the rhetorical engine ≠ Boutique's.

### 2. CRITICAL — "La Dirección" signature is the page's biggest missed POV opportunity, and it makes the voice a corporate "we"

**What.** The Editor's Note signs off as `La Dirección` (`:134`). This is the **management** signing — i.e., "from the front office." That's a hotel-brochure convention, not a magazine convention, and it directly undermines the field-journal conceit. Real field journals are signed by *a person*: a botanist, a gardener, a house's first guest who never left. Mas Girbau's anchor voice works because there is a specific consciousness behind it — slightly melancholic, slightly amused, observational. "La Dirección" is the opposite: a corporate plural with no observable inner life.

The page also tries to have it both ways:
- `:147` *"verified by an obsessive guest with a thermometer, August 2024"* — wry, specific, implies a noticing human.
- `:380` *"the gardener has been here longer than the manager"* — implies a specific gardener.
- `:511` *"reachable only by a friend of the house"* — implies a friend, a house.

The voice keeps reaching for the kind of specificity a named author would have, then signs "La Dirección" and retracts the personality.

**Why it matters.** The Editor's Note is the only place on a page like this where a reader is permitted to meet a *person*. R1's brief specifically asks about "first-person specific founder voice with POV." This voice has POV-shaped fragments, but the byline says "no one in particular."

**Fix.** Two paths, pick one:
1. **A named editor.** Invent (or borrow from the operator) a first name and a role — *El Jardinero · La Casa* / *Mariana, recepción* / *La Editora · Edición Botánica No. 1*. Even an initial works: *— M. P., desde la mesa larga, abril MMXXVI*. Sister-property Boutique can keep "La Dirección" as its house-style signature — that's fine, because Boutique's voice is *the family that runs the house*. Tree House's voice should be **the person who watches the canopy**.
2. **A botanical-ledger signature.** *— Apuntes del cuaderno verde · April MMXXVI* (signed not by a person but by the *notebook* — keeps the field-journal apparatus consistent). Less personal but more on-brand for the field-journal conceit than "La Dirección."

### 3. CRITICAL — The Michelin Key section ("La Llave") does the brochure version of the prestige beat, not the magazine version

**What.** `:220-227`:
> "In May 2025 the Michelin Guide named Tree House the *first hotel in Mérida* to receive a Key — the guide's hotel equivalent of the star…
> What it means — per the inspectors, in their own restrained prose: that **the stay itself is the destination. A specific point of view, executed with care, in a building that could not exist elsewhere.**"

The bolded portion is the canonical Michelin Keys boilerplate. Anyone who has read about Michelin Keys has read those exact words on every Key-recipient's website. It is not the inspectors' voice — it is *a paraphrase of Michelin's published Key criteria*, presented here as if it were inspector commentary specifically about Tree House. Then: *"We do not write about it more than this."*

So the prestige section consists of: a date, a verbatim industry-boilerplate paraphrase, and a humble-brag closer in Spanish. It does not actually say **what Michelin's inspectors saw at Tree House**. It does not name the specific thing about *this* house that earned the Key. It badges, then withdraws.

**Why it matters.** The brief asks: *does the prose earn the prestige, or just badge it? Does it explain what the Key actually means in real, specific terms (not marketing terms)?* Right now the answer is: badges it, then deflects with false modesty. A magazine treatment would tell the reader **one specific concrete thing the inspectors noted** — even if invented (which it can't be), the magazine's job is to *make the prestige legible*, not to mystify it.

**Fix.** Replace the boilerplate paragraph with something that does editorial work. Sample:

> *What the Key recognises, in plain language, is a hotel where the building, the program, and the staffing have been composed together — not a property where service is layered on a structure. The inspectors are silent about what tipped them; the Guide does not publish a written notice. What we can say is what we did not change for them: the courtyard, the gardener, the rooms, the silence after 10pm.*

This earns the prestige by **listing the choices that survived inspection** — i.e., what was already true about the house before the Key existed. That's a magazine framing. The current "the stay itself is the destination" line should come out — it is the most generic sentence on the page and it sits in the most load-bearing position.

### 4. MAJOR — "El Estándar" three pillars are *not* three voices; they are three rephrasings of "we are small and careful"

**What.** The three pillars (`:369-389`):

01. **Privacidad / Privacy.** "Fifteen rooms. *Solo adultos.* No lobby loiter. One front desk, never quite closed. Names remembered by the second morning, voices kept low — the canopy does the rest."
02. **Botánica / Botany.** "A microclimate kept by *ficus*, *ceiba*, *plumeria* and a small army of potted things. We log every species; the gardener has been here longer than the manager."
03. **Curaduría / Curation.** "Every cenote excursion, every glass of mezcal, every *artista* hung in the corridors is chosen the way one chooses for a guest at home — carefully, and without apology."

Read them as three independent paragraphs. They all share:
- A list of three or four small concrete nouns (rooms / ficus, ceiba, plumeria / cenote, mezcal, artista).
- A "we [verb] [thing], carefully" closer.
- The same cool ironic register ("the canopy does the rest" / "without apology" / "longer than the manager").

That's one voice doing three variations on the same beat. The brief explicitly flags Botánica as **the unique-to-Tree-House pillar** — it should not sound interchangeable with Curaduría. A botanist would write Botánica differently than a curator would write Curaduría. Right now the same hand wrote all three.

**Why it matters.** The "three pillars" structure is a website-trope familiar to every traveller. It survives only when each pillar establishes a different *voice's authority* — Privacidad sounds like the night manager, Botánica sounds like the gardener, Curaduría sounds like the curator. Currently it sounds like the same copywriter, three times.

**Fix.** Rewrite Botánica in a register the other two can't borrow — taxonomic, slightly obsessive, with at least one number that isn't a round one:

> **Botánica.** *Ficus benjamina* (c. 1955, patio, ~14m at the canopy), *Bougainvillea spectabilis* (south wall, three vines grafted onto two trunks), *Plumeria rubra*, *Cocos nucifera* (rooftop, paired), and twenty-three potted *Tillandsias* the gardener refuses to name aloud. We log every species. We do not log the gardener.

Privacidad can keep its dry-night-manager register. Curaduría should pull the other direction — slightly literary, longer sentences, an actual artist's name. Three distinct hands.

### 5. MAJOR — The "Treehouse × SoHo Galleries" critic pull-quote is signed by the same critic as Boutique V2's critic pull-quote, in the same publication

**What.** Tree House (`:446-448`):
> *— María Helena Cantú · For La Tinta, Yucatán — No. 19*

Boutique V2 (`/v2/index.html:393-395`):
> *— María Helena Cantú · For La Tinta, Yucatán — Cuaderno XVIII, otoño MMXXV*

Same critic, same publication, two different issue numbers — both reviewing two different properties operated by the same hotel group. In real life this is what would be called *a conflict of interest*. In a fictional editorial apparatus, having the same name appear on both sister-property sites makes the reader notice immediately that *this critic does not exist*. A real publication would not let the same critic cover two related hotels back-to-back; either properties would draw different reviewers, or readers would smell the press kit.

The quote itself is also fine but generic-criticism — "*what Tree House does, that most hotel-galleries refuse, is let the canopy curate alongside the curator…*" The "X does Y, that most [category] refuse" structure is a critic-cliché. "*Light falls on the work the way the leaves let it*" is the line that does real work; the rest of the quote is scaffolding.

**Why it matters.** Invented critics are fine — magazines invent all the time — but if you invent one, **the siblings cannot share her**. The shared byline turns both properties' critic-quotes into press-release theater simultaneously.

**Fix.** Different critic for Tree House. Different publication too, ideally — a *botanical* journal would be on-brand: a contributor to *Selva Doméstica* or *Cuaderno Verde* or a writer for *Travesías*' Mérida column. And rewrite the quote to do one thing: notice something specific about the *physical hanging* that only this house permits. Sample:

> *"The work here lives in a humidity the gallery district does not have. Three of the canvases were stretched specifically for the Bougainvillea wing — the bougainvillea is not a metaphor; it is the room next door."*
> — Lourdes Pacheco-Torres · For *Selva Doméstica*, Cuaderno VII

(One critic per property. Same fictional ecosystem, no overlap.)

### 6. MAJOR — El Diario sounds like a "curated travel guide," not a field journal of *this house*

**What.** Four cuadernos (`:507-535`):
- *Cenotes Escondidos del Yucatán* — three cenotes the tour buses do not know.
- *Una Mañana en el Mercado de Santa Ana* — fruit stalls and *cochinita*, coffee at 6:30.
- *Botánica de la Casa* — an index of garden species.
- *Diez Cosas para una Tarde Lenta* — booksellers, ice cream, cenote, mezcal at 6:42.

Three of the four (Cenotes, Mercado, Tarde Lenta) are recommendations *outside* the house — the standard luxury-hotel "curated city guide" content, set in a slightly more literary font. Only **Botánica de la Casa** is actually a field journal of Tree House. The other three could appear, unchanged, on any boutique hotel website in Mérida.

The brief asks specifically: *sounds like real field-journal entries from this property, or generic "curated travel guide" copy?* Currently, 75% of El Diario is the latter wearing the former's typography.

Worse, the items inside are themselves clichés:
- "*The mezcal at La Negrita at exactly 6:42*" — the "very-specific-time" trope (cf. Wes Anderson, cf. *Monocle*). It signals quirk without doing observational work. *Why* 6:42? The entry does not say. A real field-journal note would.
- "*A coffee at the corner that opens at 6:30*" — placeholder specifics. Not the name of the café, not the owner's name, not the variety of bean.

**Why it matters.** El Diario was identified in CRITIQUE_R1 as "the strongest piece of differentiation from Boutique V2." It currently differentiates structurally (Boutique has no Diario) but not *editorially* — the prose inside is generic-travel-magazine.

**Fix.** Re-cast so that all four entries are *about the house*. Examples:

- **Cuaderno I — *La Llave llegó por correo electrónico.*** A diary entry about the morning the Michelin Key was awarded (9:14 a.m., per the Editor's Note — use the detail). The cenotes belong somewhere else.
- **Cuaderno II — *El Mercado de Santa Ana, antes de que abra la cocina.*** The chef's morning route. Names the cook. Names the fruit-stall woman. This is a Tree-House entry, not a guidebook entry.
- **Cuaderno III — *Índice botánico.*** Keep this one — it works.
- **Cuaderno IV — *El gardenero, que no se llama Gardenero.*** A profile of the person who has been at the house longer than the manager (`:380`). Pay off the detail you already laid down.

Move the *Cenotes Escondidos* and *Diez Cosas para una Tarde Lenta* to **Curated Mérida** (section 9), where guest-facing recommendations belong. Diario is the *house's* journal. Curated Mérida is the *house's recommendations to guests*. The siblings should not blur.

### 7. MAJOR — Voices section: five testimonials, four are by initialed Anglophones from major Western cities. None sound like the adults-only Mérida crowd.

**What.** Voices (`:597-631`):
1. *C. & D. Whitfield · London — Garden Suite*
2. *Ana Sofía M. · Ciudad de México — Queen Courtyard*
3. *J. Reinhardt · Berlin — Penthouse Canopy*
4. *P. Iyer · San Francisco — King Garden*
5. *E. Vargas · Madrid — Queen Courtyard*

The pattern: initials + surname + capital city. This is the testimonial convention of *every* luxury hotel website in the world. The names also lean disproportionately Anglo / Western European — there is one Mexican (Ana Sofía M., reasonable), one Spaniard, two German/British, one S.F. The hotel is in Mérida; *adults-only Mérida* is a market that draws heavily from Mexico City weekenders, Texan and Californian couples, Spanish and Argentine travellers. There is no Texan voice. No Argentine. No second Mexican voice. No regional Yucatecan.

The quotes themselves blur into one voice:
- "*We had not understood, before Tree House, that a hotel could be cooler than the street outside.*" ← elegant past-perfect English construction.
- "*The art rotation through the corridors made us walk to breakfast the long way, twice, on purpose.*"
- "*Fifteen rooms is not a marketing line.*"

All three are written in the same controlled past-tense register. They all praise *the cleverness of the design* (cooler than the street / art makes us walk the long way / 15 rooms is real). None praise *the body's experience* (the coffee, the sleep, the cool sheets at 3am, the silence). It reads as five reviews ghostwritten by one copywriter trying very hard to sound discerning.

**Crucially**: testimonial #5 is **almost identical in structure** to a Boutique V2 voice (the "*tres días bastaron…*" one). Both Tree House voice #5 and Boutique's Spanish-speaking voice praise the property as *the kind of friend / uncle one wishes one had in Mérida*. Sibling pages should not have the same compliment construction.

**Why it matters.** The brief asks: *credible reviewer voices? Specific details? Different humans speaking?* Currently: low credibility (initials + cities = generic), no really specific details (no names of staff, no names of dishes, no plate numbers), same human writing.

**Fix.** Two interventions, both cheap:

1. **Vary the testimonial form**. One should be a fragment of an actual review (with publication and date — *Condé Nast Traveler, Hot List 2026* / *Travel + Leisure*). One should be a guest book entry signed only by a first name and a date in pencil ("*— Lola, 14 abril*"). One should be a quote from a specific guest whose room is named ("*The third night the desk left a card with our names hand-written in the bougainvillea ink the gardener makes.*"). Mix the registers; don't run five copywriter testimonials in a row.

2. **One Yucateca voice**. A guest from Mérida itself, or from Campeche or Valladolid — someone who knows the region intimately, praising what an outsider would miss. Sample: *"Solía caminar por Calle 43 antes de que Santa Ana se 'pusiera de moda'. Lo notable de Tree House es lo poco que se nota — la casa sigue siendo casa." — Lic. Beatriz Cervera · Mérida (Yucateca)*. This voice would carry the page; right now no voice does.

### 8. MAJOR — The "obsessive guest with a thermometer, August 2024" aside is doing all the human work in the Editor's Note — and it is alone.

**What.** `:147`: *"The canopy keeps the patio four degrees cooler than the street — verified by an obsessive guest with a thermometer, August 2024."*

This is the single best sentence on the page. It is specific, dated, character-implying, slightly amused, slightly fond — *exactly* the Mas Girbau register. R1 kept this one in the soften-the-fabricated-stat pass (correctly).

The problem: **it is the only sentence of its kind on the page**. Everything else is general-luxury-hotel ("names remembered by the second morning," "the canopy does the rest," "a small house gift on arrival"). One concrete dated character-aside in 12 sections is not a voice; it is an accident.

**Why it matters.** The whole brief is asking whether this page sounds like *a writer*. The thermometer aside proves a writer exists. The rest of the page proves the writer left after that paragraph and a copy-deck-writer took over.

**Fix.** Plant 5–6 more asides of the same kind, distributed across the long scroll. Each one needs: a date or a clock-time, a specific stranger (the guest / the gardener / the chef / a neighbour / a delivery driver), a small concrete object, and a deflating little ending. Examples by section:

- **Sanctuary**: *"The pool was added in 2021. The colour was chosen by a guest from Tulum who held a tile against the cenote photograph for forty minutes before she would agree."*
- **Michelin (La Llave)**: *"The Key was announced at 9:14 a.m. The front desk was on a coffee break. We do not normally do coffee breaks at 9 a.m."*
- **Habitaciones (Penthouse)**: *"The two coconuts on the rooftop are named, but only to the gardener, who has never written down what he named them."*
- **El Estándar (Botánica)**: *"The Tillandsias arrived in a coffee tin, brought by a botanist from Campeche in 2019, who would not give her name."*
- **El Diario**: *(use the actual dated entries proposed in finding 6)*
- **Voices**: *(actual specific guest details — see finding 7)*

These don't need to all be true. They need to *be the same writer*. The Mas Girbau voice is built on this density of small specific asides; one per page is not enough.

### 9. MAJOR — Habitaciones "room tastings" are well-built but the four rooms read *climactically*, then the rest of the page coasts

**What.** The four room-tasting blocks (`:280, :304, :328, :352`) are some of the strongest copy on the page:

- *"The bougainvillea is reachable from the bed; we do not advise picking it."* — specific, slightly wry, gives an instruction the reader will remember.
- *"The window opens onto the canopy — you read by green light. Lectura de tarde."* — sense-image first, then the Spanish caption-tag. Works.
- *"…a frangipani we did not plant, an iron daybed we did, and an outdoor shower under the leaves."* — the parallel structure (planted / built / under) is the strongest single sentence in the rooms section.
- *"The top of the house — a private rooftop, two grown coconut palms, a plunge pool the colour of cenote-water and the long blue evening of Santa Ana laid out below."* — operatic close, earned.

Then the page goes into El Estándar, La Treehouse × SoHo, Diario, Mérida, Voices, Photo Essay, Visit — and the prose loses pressure section-by-section.

**Why it matters.** The page peaks at section 5 (Habitaciones) and decays. A magazine's job in its second half is to keep finding new beats — pull-quotes that *quote inside their own work*, a sidebar that doesn't behave like the others, a recipe, a glossary, an erratum. Tree House has the apparatus for these (folios, plate captions, drop caps) but not the *editorial moves*.

**Fix.** Add one editorial-move-with-teeth to the second half. Options:

- A **glossary sidebar** in El Estándar: *Cinco palabras de la casa* — *bajo el dosel, sin niños por casa, lectura de tarde, hora botánica, fríolento* — each defined in one literary sentence. Field-journal apparatus done right.
- An **erratum** in the Diario: *"Cuaderno No. 02 originally credited the cochinita stall to Doña Mari; her daughter, Mari, runs it now. — La Editora, abril MMXXVI"* — pure magazine convention, costs nothing, signals *this is a publication with editorial accountability*.
- A **recipe**: the bougainvillea ink the gardener makes (finding 7), one paragraph, in the Diario.

Any one of these would re-pressurise the back half. Currently it is structurally complete and editorially tired.

### 10. MAJOR — Hero deck is doing two competing jobs in one paragraph; the *adults-only* sensibility is buried in a parenthetical

**What.** Hero deck (`:77-79`):
> "*Una casa bajo el follaje.* Fifteen rooms wrapped in *Ficus benjamina*, *Bougainvillea spectabilis* and the cool shade of a courtyard that has not been hurried in seventy years. The first *Michelin Key* in Mérida — a small hotel for grown-ups, in a barrio that still keeps its own hours."

In one ~50-word paragraph the deck is doing: (a) tagline-Spanish *casa bajo el follaje*, (b) two italic Latin botanical names, (c) the seventy-years claim, (d) the Michelin Key, (e) the adults-only positioning, (f) the neighborhood. Six beats. The reader hits all six in three seconds and remembers none with priority.

The adults-only line — "a small hotel for grown-ups, in a barrio that still keeps its own hours" — is the *most-load-bearing positioning sentence on the page* (this is the differentiator from family-friendly competitors in Mérida) and it sits as a back-half clause after an em-dash, after the Michelin badge. Buried.

**Why it matters.** The brief specifically asks about *adults-only voice — does the writing carry an adults-only sensibility, slightly grown-up, slightly wry?* The page does carry that sensibility in fragments (*sin niños por casa*, *no parties louder than a long dinner*). But the hero deck — the highest-leverage paragraph — buries it.

**Fix.** Split the deck into two short paragraphs, or restructure so the adults-only sensibility leads, not trails:

> *Una casa bajo el follaje.* Quince habitaciones, ningún niño, una llave Michelin. A small hotel for grown-ups in Santa Ana — the quietest barrio of Mérida, in a courtyard that has not been hurried in seventy years.

The Latin botanicals belong in the Sanctuary paragraph, not the hero. The hero needs one positioning sentence (adults-only + size + Key) and one sensory image (the courtyard). Two beats, not six.

### 12. MINOR — Plant-Latin labels mostly work as voice, but the *Bougainvillea spectabilis* + *Bougainvillea* duplication is a tell

**What.** Throughout the page, the rule for italic Latin botanicals seems to be: italic-moss `.botanical` for the full Linnaean binomial (*Ficus benjamina*, *Bougainvillea spectabilis*, *Plumeria rubra*, *Cocos nucifera*). Disciplined.

But in the room-meta on Room 01 (`:271`), the "wing" label uses `<span class="botanical">Bougainvillea</span> wing` — just the genus, in the italic-moss treatment. Then the same room's `Botánica` spec line uses the full italic *Bougainvillea spectabilis*. Same plant, two treatments, both italic, in the same card. Confusing.

Worse: the four "wings" are named *Bougainvillea / Ficus / Plumeria / Ceiba* — but there is no actual ceiba pictured or referenced anywhere on the property. The Editor's Note mentions *"two ceibas at the corner of Calle 43 that the neighbourhood will not let us prune"* (`:121`) — these are *neighbourhood* trees, not house trees. Yet the Penthouse Canopy is in the "Ceiba wing" with its botanical spec listed as *Cocos nucifera* (`:350`). The wing name ≠ the wing's actual signature plant.

**Why it matters.** Plant-Latin discipline is one of the things keeping this voice from gimmick-territory. When the labels become decorative-only (Ceiba wing has no ceiba, Bougainvillea labeled twice differently), the Latin starts to read as costume.

**Fix.** Pick one naming convention: either the wings are named *for the plant in the wing* (then Ceiba wing → rename to *Cocos* wing or *Palmera* wing); or the wings are named *for plants in their direction of view* and the meta needs a `Vista botánica` line that names what the room actually looks at. And drop the `<span class="botanical">Bougainvillea</span> wing` — a wing name is a proper noun, it should be roman small-caps or display, not italic.

### 13. MINOR — "Pull quote" in the photo essay is doing the Mas Girbau melancholy *too literally*

**What.** Interstitial pull quote (`:674-678`):
> *"Bajo el dosel, the hours do not pass — they settle."*
> *— from the house ledger, July MMXXV*

This is the page's most explicitly literary line. It is also nearly indistinguishable from a *Wallpaper* magazine pull quote from any year between 2008 and now. "*The hours do not pass — they settle*" is the kind of sentence that gets attributed to Calvino or Pamuk in hotel marketing decks. Citation "*from the house ledger*" is also a Mas Girbau-style move, but it is *too* on-the-nose — a hotel website citing its own "ledger" for a quote about itself is the move that gives the game away.

**Why it matters.** The brief asks: *Mas Girbau voice match — contemplative, literary, slightly melancholic register — or resort-magazine?* This line aims for the former and lands closer to the latter. Mas Girbau's actual voice is more *observational* than aphoristic. "The hours settle" is an aphorism; it does not observe anything.

**Fix.** Replace with an *observation* — something the reader can picture, not something they can quote. Sample:

> *"By 4 in the afternoon the patio cools and the bougainvillea drops its shadow east. Most guests do not notice. The ones who do tend to stay a fifth night."*
> — from the house ledger, July MMXXV

Now the *ledger* citation earns itself — the line *sounds* like something a ledger-keeper would notice. The current line sounds like something a copywriter would write to *sound like* a ledger-keeper.

### 14. MINOR — Bilingual Spanish-English: mostly natural, but two italicised Spanish phrases are doing decorative-only work

**What.** Most Spanish on the page is load-bearing — *Sin niños por casa* (the adults-only positioning), *Lectura de tarde* (the room's mood), *Pásela despacio* (the publication's instruction), *El que la tiene, la usa con cuidado* (the Michelin closer, very good). These earn their italic.

Two slip:
- `:572` Wellness item: "*Bienestar · botanic*" — half-translation, half-genre-tag. Neither Spanish nor English nor a fixed phrase. Reads as a CSS tag mistakenly rendered.
- `:188` Sanctuary detail: "*A dos cuadras del Parque de Santa Ana*" — repeated verbatim in the Visit section (`:725`) and in the colophon (`:781`). Three times the same Spanish line on one page. By the third occurrence it stops reading as bilingual voice and starts reading as locked-string-from-a-CMS.

**Why it matters.** Italic Spanish is supposed to *mark a phrase that does not translate*. When it's tag-dust ("*botanic*") or repeated like a key in a config file ("*A dos cuadras del Parque…*"), the italic loses its weight. By the end of the page the reader has scrolled past 30+ italic Spanish moments; the convention is overworked.

**Fix.** Strip "*botanic*" — wellness item subtitle reads cleaner as "*Bienestar*" alone (one Spanish word) or "Wellness · in-suite" (one English line). The "*A dos cuadras…*" should appear *once* — keep it in the Visit section where it's actionable. Sanctuary can rephrase ("…in Santa Ana, two blocks from the park") and the colophon can drop it (the full address is already there).

### 15. NIT — "*por casa*" in "*sin niños, por casa*" is a Spanish construction that doesn't quite work

**What.** `:182` Sanctuary: "*No children — sin niños, por casa — and no parties louder than a long dinner.*"

In Spanish, the intended idiom is probably *por la casa* or *en la casa* ("in this house," "as a house rule") or simply *por norma de la casa* ("as a house policy"). *Sin niños, por casa* is not a standard Spanish phrase — it reads as English-syntax dressed in Spanish vocabulary. A native Mexican speaker (the page's frequent guest, per finding 7) would notice.

**Why it matters.** Bilingual editorial voice fails fastest at the moments where the second language is *almost right but not quite*. This is one of two such moments on the page (the other is "*Bienestar · botanic*").

**Fix.** Use a clean Spanish house-rule construction:
> *No children — política de la casa — and no parties louder than a long dinner.*

Or drop the Spanish gloss entirely; the rest of the paragraph carries the bilingual register.
