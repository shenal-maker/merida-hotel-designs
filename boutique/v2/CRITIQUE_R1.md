# V2 — Editorial Magazine: Round 1 Critique
## Focus — Visual Hierarchy & Typography

A senior art-director's read of `index.html` + `css/style.css` (+ `js/main.js` where it touches type). Mas Girbau is the anchor — and the bar is "things printed in 5,000 copies, not things rendered in Figma." Findings below in roughly descending severity.

---

### 1. The "01 / Nota del Editor" section markers don't line up with the page-counter / data-section numbering — the magazine apparatus contradicts itself. **MAJOR**
- **What:** `index.html` decorates the hero as `data-section="1" data-section-label="Mérida"` (line 56) but no `01 /` marker appears in the hero itself. The Editor's Note is `data-section="2"` (line 97) yet its visible marker reads `01 / Nota del Editor` (line 100). Sanctuary is `data-section="3"` but marker is `02 /` (line 143). This off-by-one persists all the way to "08 / Ensayo Fotográfico" in `data-section="9"` (lines 532–534), and "09 / El Hotel" sits in `data-section="10"` (lines 597–598). The fixed `.page-counter` shows the data-section number, while the body shows the marker number — so a reader will literally see "02 / Mérida" in the corner while reading "01 / Nota del Editor" on the page.
- **Why it matters:** Section markers and plate numbers are the *spine* of editorial design. The moment readers notice the numbering disagrees with itself, the magazine apparatus reads as decoration rather than as a real system. Mas Girbau wouldn't ship this.
- **Fix:** Decide which is canonical. Easiest: drop the hero from the numbered sequence (it's the cover, not a section), renumber `data-section` 2→1, 3→2, …, 10→9 on the `<section>` elements; the visible markers stay as-is. Update `.page-counter-label` defaults in JS accordingly.

### 2. Hero "line-3" tucks two italic colors into a single phrase — italic chrome on italic chrome. **MAJOR**
- **What:** `index.html` line 68: `<span class="line-3 reveal-line"><em>by</em> The Museo</span>`. CSS at lines 440–452: `.hero-title .line-3` is set to `font-style: italic` *and* `color: var(--sage-deep)`, and inside it `em` is *also* italic and recolored `--ochre-deep`. So the whole phrase is italic — meaning the `<em>` adds no slant — it only adds an ochre color shift on the word "by."
- **Why it matters:** Italic is a typographic emphasis tool. When the whole line is italic, `<em>` becomes a color swatch, not a stress mark — the reader reads "*by*" as a graphic element, not as emphasis. Worse, the cover of the magazine is exactly where typographic discipline should be strictest.
- **Fix:** Either set `.hero-title .line-3` to roman (remove `font-style: italic`) and let the `em` carry the slant + ochre, or keep line-3 italic and downgrade the `<em>` to a `<span class="hero-by">` styled as small-caps Inter at 0.45em — closer to a Mas Girbau-style typographic ligature.

### 3. Drop cap is structurally broken on its only appearance. **MAJOR**
- **What:** `.drop-cap::first-letter` (CSS 249–258) targets `<p class="reveal drop-cap">` at line 106 — but the very first character of that paragraph is "W" of "We". CSS sets `font-size: 4.4em`, `line-height: 0.82`, `margin-top: 0.08em`. With Cormorant body running `line-height: 1.85` (CSS 598), a 4.4em initial at 0.82 line-height sits ~3.6em tall while the surrounding text is ~2em per line — meaning the cap occupies roughly 1.8 lines but `float: left` will wrap text around 1 line only, leaving the second line riding alongside the descender of the cap with awkward whitespace. There's no `shape-outside` and no manual line-count cap. There is also only **one** drop cap on the entire page — drop caps are a rhythmic device; appearing once reads as ornament.
- **Why it matters:** Drop caps are the most-photographed element of any magazine layout. A floating, badly-seated cap is the single thing that turns "editorial" into "Squarespace template."
- **Fix:** Set `.drop-cap::first-letter { font-size: 3.6em; line-height: 0.78; margin-top: 0.12em; padding-right: 0.08em; }` and add a sibling rule for `padding-top: 0.25rem` on the paragraph to anchor the baseline. More importantly: add drop caps to the opening paragraph of at least Sanctuary, Treehouse, and Curated Mérida so the device has rhythm. Or: remove it entirely. One drop cap is worse than zero.

### 4. The "El Estándar" dark panel has body copy at `rgba(250, 246, 238, 0.72)` — below WCAG AA on a wash of `--ink`. **MAJOR**
- **What:** CSS line 1035: `.standard-item p { color: rgba(250, 246, 238, 0.72); }` on background `--ink: #1a1410` (CSS 18, 955). That puts effective text contrast around 9:1 — actually OK on a pure ink ground — but the `.standard-deck` at line 982 uses `rgba(..., 0.65)` and the `.standard-stat-unit` (line 1056) uses `var(--stone)` = `#8a7e6b` directly on `--ink`. Stone-on-ink computes to ~3.6:1, which fails WCAG AA for body text. The same `--stone` on `--ink` issue recurs in `.visit-detail-mono` (CSS 1729 on .visit background `--ink` line 1651) and `.standard-stat-unit`. The `.visit-card p` at 0.78 alpha is borderline (~10:1, fine), but the *eyebrows* aren't.
- **Why it matters:** This is the one section that goes dark — it's the loudest moment on the page, and the moment your eye can't read the small-caps Inter under "Privacidad" you lose half the editorial conceit.
- **Fix:** On `--ink` backgrounds, swap `var(--stone)` for `rgba(250, 246, 238, 0.55)` (a desaturated ivory) for small-caps Inter. Bump `.standard-deck` from 0.65 → 0.78. Audit every place `var(--stone)` appears inside `.standard` and `.visit`.

### 5. Sanctuary three-column headline silently collapses to a left-aligned stack on tablet — but the *middle* column is the headline word, so the rhythm is destroyed. **MAJOR**
- **What:** CSS 679–716 sets `.sanctuary-headline` to `grid-template-columns: 1fr auto 1fr` with the big word ("Fifteen") centered between two small-caps phrases (`A House of` / `Rooms — only`). At `max-width: 1024px` (CSS 1972–1981) all three become `1fr`, left-aligned, in source order — so the reader sees "A House of" / "Fifteen" / "Rooms — only" stacked flush-left, which reads as three unrelated labels rather than a single sentence. The intended three-element rhythm (Mas Girbau's signature device) vanishes exactly where you most need it to survive — on a 13" laptop or iPad in landscape.
- **Why it matters:** This is the section that exists *to perform* magazine hierarchy. If it works only at >1024px it works only on the art director's screen.
- **Fix:** At tablet breakpoint keep the headline as a single composed unit. Either (a) keep `grid-template-columns: auto auto 1fr` with `align-items: baseline` so the small-caps sit beside the big word like a printed dateline, or (b) collapse to `grid-template-columns: 1fr` but use `text-align: center` and treat the three pieces as a single centered triplet with `display: block`.

### 6. Plate numbering is decorative-only — it isn't a real referencing system. **MAJOR**
- **What:** Plates I–XXI run sequentially across the document (HTML 88, 129, 157, 181, 205, 228, 251, 274, 355, 365, 393, 403, 413, 542, 547, 552, 560, 571, 579, 584, 589). Nothing in the body copy ever says "see Plate IX" or "as in Plate XIV." The Roman numerals are visible chrome; they refer to nothing.
- **Why it matters:** Real magazine plate numbers are *apparatus*, not decoration — a reader following "Pasta tiles (see Plate XVI)" understands they're reading a curated essay. Without textual reference, the Roman numerals are costume jewelry. A skeptical reader will clock this in ~30 seconds.
- **Fix:** Embed at least 4–5 plate references in body copy. E.g., Editor's Note "the corridor in Plate II"; Sanctuary "the patio at three in the afternoon (Plate III)"; Treehouse coda "the rotation currently on view in the eastern corridor (Plates X–XIII)"; Photo Essay "Plate XVI shows the original mosaic." Even three good references will sell the device.

### 7. The masthead nav text-decoration underline animates from `var(--ochre)` but the section-markers are also ochre, the page-counter label is ochre, the section-marker `--ochre`, hero meta-sep ochre, draw-line ochre, signature-mark ochre, room-meta ochre, treehouse `&times;` ochre, fullbleed-num ochre, plate-tag ochre, merida-num ochre, voices-scroll-bar ochre, voice-card `::before` ochre, masthead-logo-dot ochre, hero-scroll-hint ochre, treehouse-pullquote rules ochre, interstitial pull-quote rules ochre, cite-sep ochre, page-counter-sep stone-into-ochre — ochre is doing too many jobs. **MAJOR**
- **What:** Search the CSS — `var(--ochre)` and `var(--ochre-deep)` appear in 30+ rules covering: rules/dividers, eyebrows, section markers, plate tags, italic accents, page-counter labels, hover states, focus rings (line 68), CTA hover (line 495), drop cap. Ochre is simultaneously: structural punctuation, brand color, accent italic color, interactive affordance, and focus indicator.
- **Why it matters:** When one accent color signals "this is a navigation hover," "this is a section divider," "this is a Spanish word," and "this is a focus ring," readers stop being able to use color as a signal. The palette is disciplined but its *application* is undisciplined.
- **Fix:** Triage. Ochre should mean **one** of: (a) magazine apparatus (markers, plate numbers, dividers) OR (b) brand-as-italic (Spanish phrases, em). Pick one. Use `--sage-deep` for the other. Move focus ring to a desaturated `--earth` or `outline-color: var(--ink)` with `outline-offset: 4px`. Move `cite-sep`, `page-counter-sep`, `masthead-logo-dot` to `--stone`.

### 8. Font weight discipline is mostly invisible because nothing exercises the loaded range. **MAJOR**
- **What:** Google Fonts URL loads Cormorant in `300;400;500;600;700` ital `300;400;500;600` (HTML 10) — six roman + four italic weights, plus DM Serif Display (which only ships at 400) and Inter `300;400;500;600`. Across `style.css` I count `font-weight: 400`, `font-weight: 500`, and `font-weight: 600` in actual use (the latter only on `.colophon-section h4` and `.colophon-section p strong`). Cormorant 300 and 700 are never invoked. There is no `font-weight: 300` body or hairline pull-quote anywhere. Display weights are uniformly 400.
- **Why it matters:** You're paying ~120KB of font payload for weights you don't use, and — worse — the visual hierarchy of the page is doing all its work through *size* and *italic*, never weight. Mas Girbau uses a 300 hairline body against a 500 italic deck and the contrast is half of what sells the magazine feel.
- **Fix:** Either prune the Google Fonts URL to `0,400;0,500;1,400;1,500` (still serves everything currently used), or — better — actually use Cormorant 300 on the long body paragraphs in Editor's Note and Sanctuary, reserving 400 for callouts and 500 italic for the lede. That's the move that earns the editorial label.

### 9. "Big-word" sizing pushes Cormorant out of its competence zone and the text-rendering shows it. **MAJOR**
- **What:** `.hero-title .line-2` uses DM Serif Display at `clamp(5rem, 13vw, 14rem)` (CSS 433) — fine, that's the display face's job. But `.sanctuary-headline .big-word` (CSS 708–716) ships at `clamp(5rem, 11vw, 13rem)` using `var(--display)` (DM Serif Display), `letter-spacing: -0.03em`. DM Serif Display is a 400-only face designed for ~3–6rem; at 13rem and tight tracking the bracket joins and ball terminals start to look chunky and the diagonal of `f` collides with its own ascender curve. Worse, `.treehouse-title` at `clamp(2.8rem, 5.5vw, 5.4rem)` (CSS 1103) mixes a roman "Treehouse" with an italic "SoHo Galleries" — but the italic switches to Cormorant via the inherited `var(--display)` fallback chain (which is DM Serif Display *then* Cormorant), and DM Serif Display ships italic — so this works, but the `treehouse-and × ×` at `0.55em` becomes a tiny crippled italic glyph that reads as a typo more than a multiplication symbol.
- **Why it matters:** At >7rem DM Serif Display starts to feel template-y. The whole point of using it is the ball terminals and bracket transitions; at huge sizes those become merely big, not refined.
- **Fix:** Cap `.line-2` and `.big-word` at ~10rem (`clamp(4.5rem, 11vw, 10.5rem)`). Reduce `letter-spacing` from `-0.025em` / `-0.03em` to `-0.015em` on the largest sizes (display faces don't need negative tracking at body-set scale, only at headline scale, and DM Serif Display already has dense letterfit). For the `×` between Treehouse and SoHo, swap the italic `&times;` for a real `&#215;` glyph in roman DM Serif at 0.7em, vertical-align: baseline.

### 10. The hero "line-1" deck has 0.32em letter-spacing — that's *very* loose Inter on top of a 14rem display word, and it reads as a tagline more than a publication line. **MAJOR**
- **What:** CSS 420–429: `.hero-title .line-1` runs Inter at `clamp(0.85rem, 1.15vw, 1.05rem)`, `letter-spacing: 0.32em`, uppercase. The phrase is `A Quiet Quarterly — A Hotel of Fifteen Rooms`. At 0.32em the words start to fall apart visually (large inter-letter gaps swallow word-breaks); the em-dash at 0.32em tracking floats in a sea of whitespace. Compounding: this small-caps line is the *only* contextualizing copy before the giant "Boutique" word, so all the burden of "this is editorial, not e-commerce" sits on a phrase that has been tracked-out to illegibility.
- **Why it matters:** Mas Girbau's small-caps device works because it's tight (~0.18em) and sits in a measured column. At 0.32em across half-viewport-width it stops being typography and becomes texture.
- **Fix:** Drop letter-spacing to 0.22em (matching `.section-marker` at CSS 153). Tighten the phrase: drop "— A Hotel of Fifteen Rooms" — the giant words below already say "Fifteen." A single small-caps line of "A Quiet Quarterly · Edición No. 1" earns its scale.

### 11. The page-counter is fixed bottom-right but never disappears, including over dark `--ink` sections — and it's wearing an `ivory` chip with a translucent backdrop-filter. **MAJOR**
- **What:** CSS 105–123: `.page-counter` is `background: rgba(250, 246, 238, 0.7)` with `backdrop-filter: blur(6px)`. Over the dark El Estándar (CSS 953–956) and Visit (CSS 1649–1653) sections, this becomes a glowing ivory pill — exactly the "glass morphism" Adele has explicitly rejected (`feedback_no_glassmorphism.md`). It also competes visually with the ochre text inside it.
- **Why it matters:** Beyond the brand-rule violation, a persistent floating chip is anti-editorial. Mas Girbau page numbers sit in the *margin*, not in a floating chip. The chip says "web app," not "magazine."
- **Fix:** Drop the chip entirely. Either (a) inline-position the page counter into the margin (left or right, with no background), aligned to the section, fading in/out as you scroll past breakpoints; or (b) remove the `background` and `backdrop-filter` and let the numerals sit naked on whichever ground they pass over, switching color via JS based on the current section's `data-section-label` or a `data-tone="dark"` flag on the section.

### 12. Section-marker → headline relationship is set up correctly in CSS but broken in the Treehouse, where the marker visually drowns. **MAJOR**
- **What:** Most sections place `.section-marker` (Inter, 0.68rem, 0.28em tracking, ochre) directly above a 5–6rem display headline — exactly the magazine rhythm you want. The Treehouse section (HTML 332–341) wraps the marker *inside* `.treehouse-frontispiece-text` alongside a `.treehouse-pretitle` ("A Feature — in collaboration with"), a multi-line title, and a `treehouse-deck`. That's four typographic layers stacked: marker (Inter caps ochre) → pretitle (Cormorant italic) → title (DM Serif huge) → deck (Cormorant). The pretitle is sized at 1rem italic — bigger than the marker, but smaller than the deck. The visual order reads "italic intro / display title / deck" and the marker gets pushed back into ornament.
- **Why it matters:** Treehouse is the centerpiece feature — its hierarchy should be the *clearest* on the page, not the most layered.
- **Fix:** Drop the `treehouse-pretitle` entirely; let "In collaboration with SoHo Galleries" live as part of the title or move it to `treehouse-credits`. The marker should remain the only kicker above the display word.

### 13. The Voices section's `::before` opening curly-quote glyph is at 7rem, `opacity: 0.25`, positioned `top: 0.5rem; left: 1.2rem` — and it overlaps the small-caps `voice-author` cite at the bottom of the previous card on horizontal scroll. **MINOR**
- **What:** CSS 1484–1494. At `flex: 0 0 min(80vw, 620px)` (CSS 1474) and `gap: 3.5rem`, the giant ochre quote glyph can overhang the previous card's right edge when in transit during a momentum scroll. The glyph is also a curly *open* quote `\201C` placed at the top-left of every card — which is correct typography for an opening quote, but the cite below has no closing-quote counterpart, so visually each card reads as "opening a quote and never closing it."
- **Why it matters:** Small issue but breaks the magazine convention of matched quote marks.
- **Fix:** Either add a tiny closing-quote glyph at the figcaption's `::before` at much smaller scale, or — better — remove the giant decorative quote entirely; the italic Cormorant `.voice-quote` already reads as a pull-quote without needing the giant ochre crutch.

### 14. The `.editorial-img` filter chain on every image is heavy-handed — `filter: saturate(0.88) sepia(0.04) brightness(0.99)` — and it homogenizes a brand built on real photography of real limestone. **MINOR**
- **What:** CSS 202–209. Every single image on the page gets a global desaturation + sepia tint, until hover, which restores. The intent is "editorial paper" but the effect is that the bougainvillea (HTML 569), cenote turquoise (HTML 577), and the linened bed (HTML 558) all read as the same dull ochre wash until you mouse over them. On mobile (no hover), they never restore.
- **Why it matters:** The palette is already ivory-cream-ochre — adding a sepia filter on top desaturates the only places (the photos themselves) where chromatic variety could earn its keep. The Mas Girbau anchor uses *full-color* photography against an ivory page.
- **Fix:** Reduce the filter: `filter: saturate(0.95) brightness(0.98)` — drop sepia entirely. Keep the hover restoration. On `@media (hover: none)` set `filter: saturate(0.95) brightness(0.98)` as the default (no need to restore on touch).

### 15. The colophon "Navegar" list uses `.colophon-section a` set in Cormorant at 1.02rem with `line-height: 2` — and the underline-grow hover sits at `bottom: 0.35rem` (CSS 1840), which produces a visible gap between text and underline that looks like a CSS bug, not an intentional choice. **MINOR**
- **What:** CSS 1822–1850. Cormorant body at line-height 2 in a column of nav links — that's *very* loose for navigation. The hover underline at `bottom: 0.35rem` floats below the text descender by about half a line, giving a "the rule belongs to the next line" feel.
- **Why it matters:** Footers are where readers test the design's last decisions. A misaligned hover rule reads as inattention.
- **Fix:** Reduce `.colophon-section a { line-height: 1.5; }` and set `a::after { bottom: 0.05rem; }` so the rule sits flush under the descender. Or, if the loose line-height is intentional for editorial whitespace, the rule needs `bottom: -0.15rem` to clear the descender uniformly.

### 16. The page-counter currently can show "10 / Visitar" — but JS pads `.padStart(2, '0')` on numbers ≥ 10, producing "10" not "X" — inconsistent with the Roman numeral plates and the MMXXVI year line. **NIT**
- **What:** `main.js` line 123: `const num = best.target.dataset.section.padStart(2, '0');`. Sections go up to 10. So the counter reads "01" through "10" — Arabic numerals — while plates use Roman (Plate I–XXI) and the year is MMXXVI. Three different numeral systems on one page.
- **Why it matters:** Inconsistency. Magazines generally pick one numbering system and commit.
- **Fix:** Convert the page-counter to Roman in JS (small lookup map I–X), or — easier — keep section count to 9 and use the cover as un-numbered. (This dovetails with finding #1.)

## What's working
- **Color palette discipline at the value level**: ivory / limestone / sage / ochre / earth / ink / cenote is genuinely cohesive, and the rejection of gold for ochre-deep is correctly applied; no smuggled blues or pinks. The sin is in *application frequency*, not in the swatches themselves.
- **`.draw-line` rules with `scaleX(0)` transform-origin: left animation**: A small editorial detail done right — the rules feel drawn, not stamped.
- **The "Plate" caption microtype on `.img-caption`**: Inter 0.66rem, 0.18em letter-spacing, ivory background, stone color — that's an accurate Mas Girbau caption.
- **The `--feature` visit card with its ochre top-border and tinted ivory wash**: clean way to signal one of the four cards is the conversion target without using a button.
- **The reading-progress spine at `left: 1.5rem`** is a smart editorial flourish — a literal book spine. Don't lose it in revisions.
- **Spanish-English bilingual rhythm in body copy** (e.g., HTML 110 `Quince habitaciones`, HTML 162 `Un refugio`, HTML 344 `Vivir dentro de la exposición`) is doing real work — italics on the Spanish, color shift to ochre-deep — that's the kind of typographic move that earns the editorial conceit.
