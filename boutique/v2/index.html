01 / Nota del Editor

From the Editor's
Note

We did not set out to build a hotel. We set out to keep a house — a long colonial home on Calle 43, with its high ceilings and inner courtyard, its bougainvillea and its hush — from disappearing into the next thing.

What grew from that decision was, against our better instincts, an inn of fifteen rooms. Quince habitaciones. Not fourteen, not sixteen. Enough to keep the city out, and the silence in — a corridor of which is shown opposite, in Plate II.

The edition you are reading is our first — an unhurried introduction to the house, to the rooms, to the artists we now live alongside through our collaboration with SoHo Galleries, and to the small, deliberate Yucatán that gathers around our front gate. The second edition is already being assembled, around the September rotation of the art program.

Stay a little. Read slowly. Bienvenida.

— Beatriz S. Aldama Editora de esta edición · Boutique by The Museo

02 / El Santuario
A House of Fifteen Rooms — only
Plate III — Escalera, ala oriente

Un refugio. A sanctuary in the limestone heart of Mérida — and, we discovered three months in, mostly an exercise in what to leave alone.

The house dates to the late nineteenth century — a Montejo-era home with a deep zaguán, a tiled courtyard, and the high cool ceilings the Yucatán demands of its architecture. We spent three years on it. By the end, the list of what we had removed was longer than the list of what we had put in.

What we kept: the original pasta tiles. The bone of the walls. The proportions of the doorways. The shade of the central patio at three in the afternoon (we photograph it in Plate XIV, later in this edition), when the city outside is at its loudest and the courtyard is at its most quiet.

What we added: linen, a small library, a quiet pool, an obsessive front desk, and the kind of staffing ratio — nearly one to one — that allows the house to feel less like a hotel and more like a private home one is fortunate enough to be lent for a week.

The Standard

Three principles, in the order we hold them.

01
Privacidad

Privacy

Fifteen rooms. One check-in desk. No lobby. By the second morning the staff know how you take your coffee — and by the third, they have stopped asking.

15 habitaciones
02
Patrimonio

Heritage

The house was built in 1898, in the long Montejo years, by a henequen family whose name is now legible only on the cedar lintel above the kitchen. We spent three years restoring what they left behind — the pasta-tile floors, the cedar beams, the mosaic of the lower patio. The artisans who came back to do the work are the same ones who keep the rest of Centro Histórico standing.

05 / La Exposición

The Treehouse × SoHo Galleries

Vivir dentro de la exposición. Not visit it, not pass through it — live inside it, for the length of a stay. A rotating collaboration between our gallery space — The Treehouse — and SoHo Galleries Mérida, in which contemporary work hangs throughout the house: the corridors, the suites, the courtyard (Plates X–XIII). Guests are not visitors to the collection. For a week, they are quietly its guardians.

06 / Curated Mérida

Curated Mérida

A short, edited guide — only the things we would tell a friend.

  1. i.

    Luna de Miel

    Honeymoon Package

    This is the one we built first, and it is still the one we get the most letters about. Champagne on arrival, a courtyard dinner the chef plates herself, a sunrise breakfast on the rooftop, and a half-day at a cenote we keep quietly to ourselves. Four nights minimum — anything shorter does the trip a disservice.

  2. ii.

    Cenotes & Haciendas

    A Day Outside the City

    A driver, a guide who has done this for fifteen years, a basket lunch from the kitchen here. Two cenotes the tour buses do not know about (one of them in Plate XIX) and one restored hacienda that still keeps its henequen press in working order. Home by sundown — there is a drink we want you to have on the roof when you get back.

  3. iii.

    In-Suite Wellness

    Bienestar

    We borrow a curandera from a family in Ticúl for the temazcal — she travels with her own stones. Massage in the room. A quiet yoga hour on the rooftop, an hour before the city is fully awake; we’ll bring up coffee after, if you want it.

  4. iv.

    Private Transfers

    From the Airport

    Fifteen minutes from MID, by a driver who knows the house and the gate code. We include it on stays of six nights or more — we call it the Resident’s Allocation and we mean it: you live here for the week.
