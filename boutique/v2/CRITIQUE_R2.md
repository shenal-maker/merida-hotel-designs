# V2 — Editorial Magazine: Round 2 Critique
## Focus — Copy, Narrative Voice, Editorial Storytelling

Round 1 fixed the apparatus (numbering, plates, hierarchy). Round 2 reads what's *between* the apparatus — sentence by sentence, against the Mas Girbau anchor and the brief's "Paz, historia y silencio" register. The bar for an editorial-magazine variant is: it should sound like one writer with a sustained voice and a point of view, not a hotel website wearing a magazine costume.

Findings below, roughly descending severity.

---

### 1. The Editor's Note signs off as "La Dirección" — and that single choice unravels the whole first-person conceit. **CRITICAL**

- **What:** The note opens "We did not set out to build a hotel. We set out to keep a house" (line 107) — a strong, intimate, founder-voice opening — but the signature block (lines 118–122) reads:
  > — La Dirección
  > Boutique by The Museo · Mérida

  "La Dirección" is corporate-speak. It is what a Mexican bank, a condominium administrator, or a hospital uses to sign a notice about elevator maintenance. It is the *opposite* of a magazine editor's note. Mas Girbau would never. The whole "We did not set out to build a hotel" gesture is staked on a real human voice; signing it "Management" detonates that.
- **Why it matters:** An editor's note is a person. The conceit of this section — and arguably of the whole variant — is that there's a named editor with a point of view. Signing "La Dirección" tells the reader "this is marketing copy that wants to feel personal but isn't." Every other voice move on the page is now suspect.
- **Fix:** Sign with a single person. Even an invented one is better than the corporate plural — but ideally use the actual founder's name. E.g.:
  > — *Tomás Aldama* (or whichever name is real)
  > *Editor de esta edición · Boutique by The Museo*

  Bonus move: italicize the role ("Editor de esta edición") — the apparatus reading the apparatus. Drop the redundant "Mérida" since the masthead already says it three times. If the founders prefer not to name a person, the second-best move is `— Los fundadores` or `— La casa` — both warmer than "La Dirección."

### 2. "Plate VII — Grand Boutique Suite" and the other room captions are not doing magazine-caption work. They are labels. **CRITICAL**

- **What:** Room captions read:
  > Plate V — Deluxe Boutique Room
  > Plate VI — Deluxe Boutique Suite
  > Plate VII — Grand Boutique Suite
  > Plate VIII — Penthouse Suite

  (lines 205, 228, 251, 274). The plate number is doing real apparatus work — but the caption text itself is just the product name. Compare to the *actually editorial* captions elsewhere on the page: "Plate III — Escalera, ala oriente" (line 157) or "Plate XIV — Patio, tres de la tarde" (line 542). Those orient the reader in space and time. The room captions tell you what marketing already told you.
- **Why it matters:** Captions are the second-most-read text on any magazine page (after headlines). When five plates in a row caption themselves with the product SKU, the editorial voice collapses into a product catalog. The plate-number device, R1's hard-won apparatus win, becomes ornament again *specifically in the rooms grid* — which is the longest single block on the page.
- **Fix:** Re-caption with editorial specificity. Each caption should name the *thing in the photograph*, not the room category. Suggestions:
  - Plate V → *"Plate V — Habitación N° 03, contraluz de la mañana"*
  - Plate VI → *"Plate VI — Suite, las puertas de cedro original"*
  - Plate VII → *"Plate VII — Suite mayor, terraza al jacaranda"*
  - Plate VIII → *"Plate VIII — Azotea, hora del encendido"* (the lamps lighting up — matches the "as the streetlamps come on" copy below)

  The room *name* already sits in the `.room-meta` row immediately below; the caption is free to do orienting work.

### 3. The "critic pull-quote" by María Helena Cantú is the best line of copy on the page — and one phrase gives away that it's invented. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Line 374–376:
  > "What is unusual is not that a hotel keeps art — many do. *What is unusual is that the art keeps the hotel.* The work does not decorate the house; it instructs it — in what hours to be quiet, in which corridors to leave the lights low."

  The chiasmus ("hotel keeps art / art keeps the hotel") is genuinely sharp — this is the strongest sentence in the variant. But the second sentence ("does not decorate / instructs it... in what hours to be quiet, in which corridors") slips out of art-critic register and into hotel-brand register. A real art critic writing for *La Tinta Yucatán* would not write "in what hours to be quiet" — that is the hotel's own language ("the curated hush of a city that whispers," line 75; "keep the silence in," line 110). The critic has been ventriloquized by the marketing.
- **Why it matters:** The whole point of the pull-quote is that it sounds like *not the hotel*. The moment the critic's voice matches the hero deck's voice, readers (especially skeptical ones — and luxury hotel buyers are skeptical) read it as fabricated. The critic should sound like she has her own preoccupations that happen to land on the hotel; she shouldn't sound like she's been hired.
- **Fix:** Rewrite the second sentence in a more *critical* register — drier, slightly skeptical, with a specific observation the hotel itself wouldn't make:
  > "What is unusual is not that a hotel keeps art — many do. *What is unusual is that the art keeps the hotel.* The Manzón bronze in the library alcove is doing the work three signs and a velvet rope would otherwise do; you lower your voice without being asked."

  That sentence (a) names a specific piece (which the captions below already cite — Plate XIII), (b) makes a wry observation about hospitality theater the hotel itself would never copy-write, (c) credits the *art* not the hotel for the effect. That's what a real critic writes.

  Also: the publication name *La Tinta* is fine, but "No. 18" with no month and no year is too vague to be plausible. Make it *La Tinta, Yucatán — Cuaderno XVIII, otoño MMXXV* or similar — give the citation the weight of a real one.

### 4. El Estándar's three pillars are written in three slightly different registers — but none of them is a *distinct editorial voice* on its principle. They are three rephrasings of "intimate luxury." **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 308, 315, 322:
  > **Privacidad:** "Fifteen rooms, a single check-in desk, no lobby loiter. Staff outnumber guests; names are remembered by the second morning. The house keeps its own counsel."
  >
  > **Patrimonio:** "A late nineteenth-century home, restored bone by bone. Original *pasta* floors, cedar beams, mosaic tilework — preserved by the same artisans who keep the colonial city standing."
  >
  > **Curaduría:** "Every artwork, every cenote excursion, every glass of mezcal at sundown is chosen the way one chooses for a guest at home — with attention, and without apology."

  All three are written with the same prose engine: a short concrete-noun list, an em-dashed coda, an aphoristic close. Same rhythm, same length, same posture. The Mas Girbau "Piedra / Hogar / Madera" trio works because each pillar is its *own* sentence-engine — one short, one elaborated, one almost a koan. Here the three are decoratively distinct (different Spanish words, different stats) but rhetorically identical.
- **Why it matters:** Three pillars in the same voice read as one idea repeated three times. The pillar device is supposed to *open* the brand into three angles — heritage is different from privacy is different from curation. The copy should *demonstrate* that difference by sounding different.
- **Fix:** Differentiate by register.
  - **Privacidad** → keep aphoristic, but shorter. *"Fifteen rooms. One check-in desk. No lobby. By the second morning, the staff know how you take your coffee — and by the third, they have stopped asking."*
  - **Patrimonio** → lengthen and slow down — heritage *takes time to describe*. *"The house was built in 1898, in the long Montejo years, by a henequen family whose name is now legible only on the cedar lintel above the kitchen. We spent three years restoring what they left: the pasta-tile floors, the cedar beams, the mosaic of the lower patio. The artisans who returned to do the work are the same ones who keep the rest of Centro Histórico standing."* (Now patrimonio reads like a tiny history — which is what it is.)
  - **Curaduría** → make it a single sharp sentence with a point of view. *"Curation is the part of hospitality that gets overused as a word; we mean it the way a museum means it — that someone with taste, and a name, and an opinion, has made the choice."*

  Now the three pillars actually behave like three different pillars.

### 5. The Sanctuary "feature" reads as a section, not a feature article — there's no thesis the prose is developing, just descriptors layered three deep. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 161–172. The structure is:
  - Para 1 (lede): "Un refugio. A sanctuary in the limestone heart of Mérida." (one line)
  - Para 2 (drop cap): "The house dates to the late nineteenth century — one of the long Montejo-era homes..." (descriptive setup)
  - Para 3: "What we kept: the original *pasta* tiles. The bone of the walls. The proportions of the doorways. The shade of the central patio at three in the afternoon (see Plate XIV)..."
  - Para 4: "What we added: linen, a small library, a quiet pool, an obsessive front desk..."

  Para 3 + Para 4 (the "what we kept / what we added" diptych) is a real editorial move — it's the closest thing to a thesis the section has. But the thesis is buried, and the lede ("Un refugio. A sanctuary in the limestone heart of Mérida") is just brand-tagline-with-Spanish-translation. There is no *argument* being made. A magazine feature would say: "Restoring a Mérida hacienda is mostly a question of subtraction" — and then spend three paragraphs showing it.
- **Why it matters:** A homepage section can describe. A magazine feature has a *point*. The brief asks for an editorial-magazine variant; the Sanctuary is its centerpiece feature. Right now it's well-written description without a spine.
- **Fix:** Promote the "what we kept / what we added" diptych to the *thesis* and rewrite the lede to set up that thesis. Sample:
  > **Lede (replacing line 162):** *Un refugio.* Most hotel restorations are an exercise in addition — what to put in. Ours, we discovered three months in, was almost entirely an exercise in what to leave alone.
  >
  > **Drop-cap paragraph (line 164–166):** [keep mostly as-is, but trim] The house dates to the late nineteenth century — a Montejo-era home with a deep *zaguán*, a tiled courtyard, and the high ceilings the Yucatán demands of its architecture. We spent three years on it. By the end, the list of what we had *removed* was longer than the list of what we had added.
  >
  > **Para 3 (what we kept) and Para 4 (what we added)** can then stay close to their current form — they are now in service of an argument the lede made.

  The section now reads like a feature: *thesis → evidence → evidence*. Same wordcount, an order of magnitude more editorial.

### 6. The "Voices" testimonials are well-cast but two of the five strain credibility — and one of those is the only Spanish-speaking voice. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 487–520. Five voices:
  - **C. & D. Whitfield, London** — "a house we had been quietly missing" — *credible*
  - **Ana Sofía M., Ciudad de México** — "the kind of thing that makes you walk to breakfast the long way, twice, on purpose" — *credible, and good*
  - **J. Reinhardt, Berlin** — "By the third morning the front desk knew the temperature of our coffee. *Por su nombre, todo.*" — A Berliner code-switching into Spanish in a guest book? Possible but precious. The *Spanish phrase itself* is also slightly off — "por su nombre, todo" doesn't quite idiom; "todo por su nombre" or "cada uno por su nombre" would land more naturally.
  - **P. Iyer, San Francisco** — "the best day my husband and I have had outside of our own wedding" — *fine, but compares-to-wedding is the single most-used trope in luxury hotel testimonial copy*. Reads marketing-written.
  - **E. Vargas, Madrid** — "The art collaboration is a real one — not a flourish." — This is the *hotel's* defensive line ("it's substantive, not bolted on") put into a guest's mouth. The hotel is dictating its own rebuttal. **This is the one that screams marketing.**
- **Why it matters:** A guest book entry that pre-empts the brand's own objection is the tell. Skeptical readers (and again, luxury buyers *are* skeptical) clock fabricated testimonials by exactly this move — the voice that confirms what the brand wants you to believe, in the brand's own framing.
- **Fix:**
  - **Cut the E. Vargas testimonial entirely**, or rewrite so it's about something *the hotel hasn't already told you*. Sample: *"E. Vargas, Madrid — Deluxe Room: 'The Pacheco henequen study in our room kept me up an extra hour on the first night. I have never written down a room number to remember a painting before.'"* — now it's praise the hotel couldn't have written for itself, with a specific reference to the art (Plate XII, line 403) — apparatus weaving through.
  - **Repair the J. Reinhardt Spanish:** *"By the third morning the front desk knew the temperature of our coffee. Cada huésped por su nombre — todos."* (or drop the Spanish tag entirely; the line works without it).
  - **Soften the P. Iyer wedding comparison:** *"P. Iyer, San Francisco — Grand Suite: 'The cenote day was, plainly, the day of the trip. We did not see another visitor — only the driver, who left us to it for an hour.'"* (Specific, doesn't trade on the wedding cliché.)

  Also: consider adding a Mexican voice that isn't a hotel-curated one. A line from a Yucatecan guest in *Spanish only, untranslated*, would earn the bilingual conceit far more than the current ratio of one Spanish phrase per English-language quote.

### 7. Curated Mérida reads as four feature listings, not four editorial entries with a point of view. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 440–471, four items: Luna de Miel, Cenotes & Haciendas, In-Suite Wellness, Private Transfers. The format is `<h3>name</h3> + <span>tag</span> + <p>description</p>`. The deck above promises: "A short, edited guide — only the things we would tell a friend" (line 434). But the entries do not read like *things told to a friend* — they read like service listings. Compare:
  - *Current:* "Champagne on arrival, a private dinner in the courtyard, a sunrise breakfast on the rooftop, a half-day at a quiet cenote with no one else in it. Four nights minimum." (line 445)
  - *What a friend says:* "We do this one a lot — the cenote alone is worth it, and there's a cenote outside Cuzamá we've never told anyone else about. Four nights at minimum; ask for it when you book."

  The current copy is *good* hotel package copy. It is not *editorial* copy.
- **Why it matters:** The deck makes a promise about voice ("the things we would tell a friend") and the entries break it. This is the section where the editor-as-narrator should be most present, and it is the section where they vanish entirely.
- **Fix:** Inject first-person aside into each entry. Two examples (the others follow the same pattern):
  > **i. Luna de Miel — Honeymoon Package:** This is the one we built first and it is still the one we get the most letters about. Champagne on arrival, a courtyard dinner the chef plates herself, a sunrise breakfast on the roof, and a half-day at a cenote we keep to ourselves. Four nights minimum, because anything shorter does the trip a disservice.
  >
  > **ii. Cenotes & Haciendas — A Day Outside the City:** A driver, a guide who has done this for fifteen years, a basket lunch from the kitchen here. Two cenotes the tour buses do not know about (one of them in *Plate XIX*) and one restored hacienda that still keeps its henequen press in working order. Home by sundown — there is a drink we want you to have on the roof when you get back.

  Note the small voice-moves: "we built first," "we keep to ourselves," "there is a drink we want you to have." That is the friend-voice the deck promised.

### 8. The Photo Essay introduction sentence undoes its own editorial conceit by reading like a captions index. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 534–535:
  > Una casa *en pausa*
  > A short visual essay — eight plates from the house and the city around it. The original mosaic appears in *Plate XVI*; bougainvillea, *Plate XVIII*; the west shutter, *Plate XXI*.

  The R1 reviser added the plate-reference sentence to make the apparatus real. But the *form* of the sentence — semicolon-separated list of `[noun], Plate [N]` — reads as a captions index at the front of a book, not a magazine feature. It's also redundant: the captions themselves already say "Mosaico original" (Plate XVI), "Bugambilia, muro sur" (Plate XVIII), "Postigo, ala oeste" (Plate XXI). The intro tells you what you're about to read instead of *framing* what you're about to read.
- **Why it matters:** A photo-essay intro should give the essay a *thesis*, the way an editor introduces a contributor. "Una casa en pausa" — *a house at pause* — is a beautiful title, and it is doing nothing because the intro doesn't earn it.
- **Fix:** Rewrite the intro to deliver on its title. Sample:
  > **Una casa *en pausa***
  > A house at pause. The Yucatán afternoon is the part of the day this house was built for: the courtyard cools, the shutters are pulled to halfway, the city outside thins. These eight plates were made between two and five in the afternoon, on three separate days. Nobody is in any of them on purpose. — *The Editors*

  That sets a *time* (afternoon), a *premise* (the house is at its most itself in those three hours), and a *house style* (no people on purpose). Now the photo essay has an argument the photos can confirm or undermine. Drop the "Plate XVI, Plate XVIII, Plate XXI" enumeration — let the captions do their own work.

### 9. The hero deck is doing too many jobs and the last sentence reaches for "literary" and lands on "real-estate listing." **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 73–76:
  > *Un refugio.* A sanctuary of limestone, courtyard and shade on Paseo de Montejo — fifteen rooms restored within the bones of a colonial home, steps from Palacio Cantón. **Privacy, patrimonio, and the curated hush of a city that whispers.**

  The first two sentences are fine — they give the reader *place* and *scale*. The third (bolded for emphasis on the page) is the problem:
  - "Privacy, patrimonio" — the pillar trio is *Privacidad / Patrimonio / Curaduría*; the hero deck switches Spanish/English inconsistently with the section below.
  - "the curated hush of a city that whispers" — this is the line that fails. "Curated hush" is a compound luxury-marketing phrase ("curated" + abstract noun is a tell); "a city that whispers" is the cliché-version of the Mas Girbau register, not the register itself. Mas Girbau's actual line — "Paz, historia y silencio" — is *plainer* than this. Three nouns, no adjectives, no metaphor. This deck reaches for the same effect with twice the words and lands softer.
- **Why it matters:** The hero deck is the first prose the reader meets. If it's wearing its literary intentions on its sleeve, every line after it has to defend itself harder. "Paz, historia y silencio" works because it doesn't try.
- **Fix:** Rewrite the third sentence in the *register the brief actually points at*. Sample:
  > *Un refugio.* A sanctuary of limestone, courtyard and shade on Paseo de Montejo — fifteen rooms restored within the bones of a colonial home, steps from Palacio Cantón. **Privacidad. Patrimonio. Silencio.**

  Three nouns, no verbs, in Spanish. The hero deck now *cites the Mas Girbau move* explicitly instead of paraphrasing it. The pillar section below picks them up (and adds *Curaduría* as the third — small variation, intentional). The hero stops trying to be literary and becomes literary by getting out of its own way.

### 10. The "Plate XIV" reference in Sanctuary is the most earned plate-citation on the page — and it points to an image the reader hasn't seen yet, far below. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Sanctuary body, line 168:
  > "...the shade of the central patio at three in the afternoon (see *Plate XIV*), when the city outside is at its loudest and the courtyard is at its most quiet."

  *Plate XIV* is the courtyard-at-3pm photograph in the photo essay (line 542). The cross-reference is *conceptually perfect* — the prose names a specific time of day, the plate caption ("Patio, tres de la tarde") confirms it, the photo essay later delivers it. But the reader meets the reference in Section 02 (Sanctuary) and the image they're being pointed to lives in Section 08 (Photo Essay) — five sections and several thousand pixels of scroll away. The pointer is real but the payoff is structurally orphaned.
- **Why it matters:** This is the *best* plate-reference on the page and it is structurally undermined. A skeptical reader who actually follows the pointer (Cmd-F "Plate XIV") gets there eventually; a normal reader scrolls past it without ever cashing the reference in.
- **Fix:** Two options.
  - **(a)** Move *Plate XIV* (the patio-at-3pm photograph) into the Sanctuary section itself — replace the current Plate III or Plate IV with it. The cross-reference now sits next to the image it names. The plate numbering can stay non-sequential within sections (real magazines do this — plates are ordered by *referencing*, not by section order). This is the better fix.
  - **(b)** Keep the structural separation but make the cross-reference *acknowledge the wait*. Sample: "the shade of the central patio at three in the afternoon (we photograph it in *Plate XIV*, later in this edition)" — the parenthesis is now a magazine editor pointing forward through their own publication. That move is itself editorial.

### 11. The "Apunte" aside next to the Editor's Note is a wasted lyrical slot. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 131–134:
  > **Apunte**
  > Fifteen rooms. *Quince.* A number chosen so that every guest would be known by name before the second breakfast.

  This is well-written but it's *the same point the body paragraph two inches to its left just made*: "an inn of fifteen rooms. *Quince habitaciones.* Not fourteen, not sixteen. Enough to keep the city out, and the silence in" (line 110). The aside repeats the fifteen-rooms beat without adding anything — it's redundancy where the magazine convention expects *new information at a different angle*.
- **Why it matters:** Marginal asides in real magazines are where editors say the thing the body copy was too dignified to say — a tangent, a behind-the-scenes detail, a citation, a footnote-with-attitude. This one is just the body copy in miniature. The asider has nothing to add.
- **Fix:** Replace with something the body copy *would not* say. Three sample directions:
  > **(a) — a behind-the-scenes detail:** *Apunte:* The smallest of our rooms was originally a sewing room. We kept the window. — *La Redacción*
  >
  > **(b) — a sourced quotation:** *Apunte:* "A house is a machine for slowing down in." — *attributed to the architect of the restoration, 2024*
  >
  > **(c) — an editorial confession:** *Apunte:* We considered sixteen rooms. The sixteenth would have required closing the small library on the second floor. We chose the library.

  Any of these adds a *new fact* the reader didn't have. That's what an apunte is for.

### 12. The Treehouse deck's drop cap opens with "Vivir dentro de la exposición. To live inside the exhibition." — and the immediate translation flattens the whole device. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Line 343 (Treehouse deck, drop cap):
  > *Vivir dentro de la exposición.* To live inside the exhibition. A rotating collaboration between our gallery space — *The Treehouse* — and SoHo Galleries Mérida...

  The drop-cap "V" lands on *Vivir* — beautiful, the Spanish gets the entrance — and then is immediately followed by an English literal translation: *To live inside the exhibition.* That is bilingual *decoration*, not bilingual *rhythm*. A reader who reads Spanish reads the same idea twice; a reader who doesn't reads the translation and notices the Spanish was performing for them. Both responses are bad. The other Spanish-led sentences on the page do this better — line 174 ("*A pasos del Palacio Cantón*") leaves the Spanish to stand alone; line 470 ("the *Resident's Allocation*") embeds the Spanish gloss.
- **Why it matters:** Bilingual prose works when each language is doing something the other can't. Side-by-side literal translation announces the conceit instead of operating it.
- **Fix:** Drop the literal translation; let the Spanish lead and the English do *different* work. Sample:
  > *Vivir dentro de la exposición.* Not visit it, not pass through it — live inside it, for the length of a stay. A rotating collaboration between our gallery space — *The Treehouse* — and SoHo Galleries Mérida, in which contemporary work hangs throughout the house: the corridors, the suites, the courtyard (*Plates X–XIII*). Guests are not visitors to a collection. For a week, they are quietly its guardians.

  Now the English sentence is *expanding* the Spanish ("not visit, not pass through — live inside") rather than translating it. The bilingual move earns its keep.

### 13. The Summer Special copy is the only place on the page where the voice cracks fully into hotel-PR register. **MAJOR**

- **What:** Lines 638–639:
  > *Verano en Mérida.* 20% off all suites for stays between 1 June and 31 August, 2026. Combinable with the Resident's Allocation — airport transfer and a small house gift on arrival.

  "20% off all suites for stays between 1 June and 31 August, 2026" is *necessary* information — and it's also written in the exact phrasing of a hotel email newsletter. Everywhere else on the page the editorial voice is doing real work (the room copy says "an iron-framed daybed that has read more books than any of us," line 264 — that's *singing*); here, suddenly, "20% off." The voice falls off a cliff at the moment of conversion.
- **Why it matters:** Conversion copy is *exactly* where editorial-magazine variants prove themselves. The Aman, the Como, the Cap Estel — luxury hotels at the top of the market don't write "20% off." They write the same offer in a register that matches the rest of the publication.
- **Fix:** Rewrite to keep the *information* and lose the *register*. Sample:
  > *Verano en Mérida.* The Yucatán is at its most itself in June and August — the city empties of its tour groups, the cenotes are clear, and the afternoons (see *Plate XIV*) are very nearly ours. For stays between June 1 and August 31 of 2026, we are quietly offering twenty percent off every suite, combinable with the Resident's Allocation — airport transfer included, and a small house gift on arrival.

  Same offer, same dates, same percentage — but the register matches the editor's note above it. "We are quietly offering" instead of "20% off" is the entire game.

### 14. Captions across the room grid use "Habitación, contraluz" / "Escalera, ala oriente" — and they're well done — but the variant is missing the *one* caption move that would earn the whole magazine conceit. **MINOR**

- **What:** All captions on the page name the *subject* of the photograph: "Patio, tres de la tarde" (Plate XIV), "Caf´e en la cocina" (Plate XX), "Postigo, ala oeste" (Plate XXI). Good. But not one caption on the page does the *other* magazine-caption move: a one-sentence narrative line that overrides the descriptive label. Real magazines mix the two — most captions are nouns; one or two per spread are sentences. The variance is what gives the apparatus its life.
- **Why it matters:** When every caption is the same *type* of caption (noun phrase + place), the device flattens. The reader stops reading captions after the third one because they've learned the pattern. A single narrative-sentence caption per page would reset attention.
- **Fix:** Convert two or three captions to sentence form. Suggestions:
  - **Plate XV (line 547)** — currently "Calle 43, atardecer." Make it: *"Plate XV — The street the front gate opens onto, ten minutes before the streetlamps come on. Calle 43 at the end of June."*
  - **Plate XX (line 584)** — currently "Café en la cocina." Make it: *"Plate XX — The first coffee of the day is made in the small kitchen behind the courtyard, not in the bar. Front desk only knows about it on Tuesdays."*
  - Leave the rest as noun-phrase captions — variance is the device.

### 15. "Cycle N° III — Primavera / Verano MMXXVI" under the Treehouse title is a great touch — but nothing else on the page acknowledges that this is a *rotating* program. **MINOR**

- **What:** Line 347:
  > Curaduría — SoHo Galleries Mérida
  > Cycle N° III — Primavera / Verano MMXXVI

  Implies there have been two previous cycles — and there will be a Cycle IV. This is excellent apparatus. But the coda (line 422) says "The current cycle is on view through August" without naming what comes after, and the Editor's Note doesn't mention the cycle program at all. The brand is *almost* claiming a serial publication identity ("Edición No. 1," "Cycle N° III") but doesn't quite commit.
- **Why it matters:** "Edición No. 1" is a promise. "Cycle N° III" is a promise. A skeptical reader notices that both promises arrive without a visible second act and quietly downgrades both.
- **Fix:** Two small additions:
  - In the Editor's Note (around line 113), add a sentence: *"This is the first edition we have published. The second is already being assembled, around the September rotation of the art program."* — Now Edición No. 1 implies Edición No. 2, and the Treehouse cycle and the publication cycle are visibly entangled.
  - In the Treehouse coda (line 422), name what comes next: *"The current cycle (Cycle N° III, on view through August) closes with a small Friday-evening seating in the courtyard; Cycle N° IV opens in mid-September with new work from three Mexican photographers."* — Now Cycle III implies Cycle IV; the rotation is real.

### 16. The colophon brand description is the third paragraph on the page that uses "the limestone heart of Mérida." **MINOR**

- **What:** The phrase "the limestone heart of Mérida" appears in:
  - Hero deck implicitly (line 73, "sanctuary of limestone")
  - Sanctuary lede (line 162, "A sanctuary in the limestone heart of Mérida")
  - Colophon brand description (line 656, "An intimate fifteen-room house in the limestone heart of Mérida")

  Three uses of a single signature phrase, two of them word-for-word identical. The phrase is *good* — but at three appearances it stops being a signature and starts being a stock phrase. Similar issue: "A pasos del Palacio Cantón" appears four times (hero deck line 75 implicitly via "steps from," Sanctuary detail line 174, Visit Address line 614, Colophon Visitar line 666).
- **Why it matters:** Magazine writers vary their phrasings precisely *because* readers notice when the same phrase recurs. Repetition is a rhetorical move (the pillar trio, "*Un refugio*") or it's a tell that the writer ran out of ways to say the thing.
- **Fix:**
  - Keep "limestone heart of Mérida" in the *Sanctuary* lede (where it's earned by the section's argument).
  - Hero deck → "a sanctuary of courtyard and shade on Paseo de Montejo" (drop "limestone"; the room copy below carries it).
  - Colophon → "An intimate fifteen-room house behind a cedar gate on Calle 43, in the historic center of Mérida." (More specific, names a *different* architectural detail.)
  - "A pasos del Palacio Cantón" is fine at 2 appearances (Visit Address + Colophon footer — they're both metadata contexts). Drop the Sanctuary-detail occurrence (line 174) and the masthead instance — they're decorative.

### 17. The hero scroll hint says "Pase la página" — turn the page — but the page does not turn, it scrolls. **NIT**

- **What:** Line 91:
  > `<div class="hero-scroll-hint">Pase la página</div>`

  Lovely Spanish phrase, exactly right register, *literally* meaning "turn the page." But the site scrolls vertically; pages don't turn. The phrase is doing the apparatus thing (magazine = pages) but contradicting the actual interaction (scroll = continuous). On a literal-minded reading, the instruction is wrong.
- **Why it matters:** Tiny. But the editorial conceit is built on the *idea* of a magazine; if the affordance-language fights the affordance, the conceit thins.
- **Fix:** Either accept the magazine-fiction (current state — fine, this is the more interesting choice) or align with the medium: *"Continúe leyendo"* / *"Siga la página"* / *"Hacia adelante"*. My recommendation is to keep "Pase la página" — it's the more confident move. But know that's the call being made.
