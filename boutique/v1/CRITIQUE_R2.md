# V1 — Cinematic Immersive · Round 2 Critique
**Focus: Copy, Narrative Voice, Content Density, Storytelling**

Read against `index.html` post-R1. Typography/hierarchy findings deliberately out of scope.

---

### 1. "We did not build a hotel — we restored a house and then learned how to keep it whispering." — **CRITICAL**
- **What:** The Sanctuary section's load-bearing sentence: *"Fifteen rooms. One courtyard. A restored colonial estate hidden one block from Paseo de Montejo. We did not build a hotel — we restored a house and then learned how to keep it whispering. Limestone, mahogany, the slow Yucatán light."*
- **Why it matters:** This is your manifesto paragraph — the one block of copy the buyer either falls in love with or doesn't. "Learned how to keep it whispering" is the closest the page comes to having a soul. The first three fragments are great. The last fragment ("Limestone, mahogany, the slow Yucatán light.") is **three nouns with one adjective** in a tricolon that's been performed three other places on the page (the breath quote, the offer copy, the location intro). The site is becoming a stylebook of fragment-fragment-fragment. The Largo's prose has *sentences* that breathe — it doesn't just chant.
- **Fix:** Break the tricolon habit at least once in this paragraph. Replace the last fragment with a real sentence that earns its place — something the *house* would say about itself, with a specific verb. e.g.: *"Limestone walls hold the cool until afternoon. Mahogany doors that swell in August and shrink by December. The light arrives slowly, and it arrives through the courtyard first."* You've earned one indulgent sentence here. Spend it.

### 2. "There is no concierge desk. There is a person who knows your name…" — **MAJOR**
- **What:** *"There is no concierge desk. There is a person who knows your name, where you walked yesterday, and whether the cenote is worth driving to today."*
- **Why it matters:** The bones are excellent. But "knows your name" is the lowest-stakes thing in the list — every chain hotel "knows your name." It's also the cliché of boutique-hotel copy from 2008 onward. The next two clauses are good and specific. The opener is doing none of the work.
- **Fix:** Cut "knows your name." Lead with the specifics: *"There is no concierge desk. There is a person who notices you came back without your hat, who has an opinion about which cenote is worth driving to today, and who will tell you the truth about both."* Or trim harder: *"There is no concierge desk. There is a person who saw which way you walked yesterday and has an opinion about the cenote."* The voice you want is *the person* talking — slightly dry, observant, unhurried — not the marketing department reassuring you.

### 3. The three "Pillars" — Privacy / Restoration / Refuge — are positioning labels pretending to be poetry — **MAJOR**
- **What:** The numbered pillars: *"I. Privacy — Fifteen keys. No lobby traffic. The courtyard belongs to whoever is in it. / II. Restoration — Original tilework, raised ceilings, a slow architectural hand. / III. Refuge — A house to disappear into — between Palacio Cantón and the rest of the city."*
- **Why it matters:** Pillars are a *deck slide* genre. They were Mas Girbau's "Piedra / Hogar / Madera" model — three nouns from the *material world* of the house, not three corporate values ("Privacy," "Restoration," "Refuge" are all categories the buyer expects to see). "Privacy" especially is the most-marketed word in boutique hospitality; using it as a literal label flattens you. Also: "a slow architectural hand" is the kind of phrase that sounds good and means nothing — name a thing the hand did.
- **Fix:** Replace the abstract category labels with concrete tangible nouns from the house — the way Mas Girbau anchors. Examples:
  - *"I. Cal" (limestone) — "The walls hold yesterday's cool until afternoon. Fifteen keys. No lobby traffic."*
  - *"II. Madera" (wood) — "Mahogany doors that need both hands. Ceilings raised back to their 1909 line."*
  - *"III. Patio" (courtyard) — "A door between Palacio Cantón and the rest of the city. Most days, it stays closed."*
  The bilingual label *carries* meaning here; the English does the storytelling. This is your single biggest opportunity to differentiate.

### 4. Hero tagline — "A courtyard. Fifteen rooms. The hush before sunrise." — promise vs. delivery audit — **MAJOR**
- **What:** The 800ms-eye-impression copy.
- **Why it matters:** "The hush before sunrise" is genuinely good — it's the only phrase on the page that owns a *time of day*. But the whole site then never returns to that hour. Where is sunrise actually in the body copy? The Sanctuary talks about "afternoon light" and "Yucatán light"; the curated section mentions "a cenote at dawn"; testimonials get "last light of day." Sunrise — the promise — is orphaned. The hero promises a film and the rest delivers a postcard.
- **Fix:** Either change the hero tagline so it makes a promise the rest delivers (e.g. *"A courtyard. Fifteen rooms. The slow Yucatán afternoon."* — matches what's actually written elsewhere), OR plant sunrise into at least two other sections so the hero's whisper has a *callback*. Specifically: the Sanctuary paragraph could end at sunrise; a testimonial could mention the hour the courtyard is loudest with birds; the Penthouse room copy could own the sunrise view explicitly. Threading > re-stating.

### 5. Room one-liners — three out of four are excellent; one is corporate — **MAJOR**
- **What:**
  - N° 01 Deluxe Boutique Room: *"The first room. Compact, considered, courtyard-adjacent."*
  - N° 02 Deluxe Boutique Suite: *"A sitting room. A second light. Mornings stretch."*
  - N° 03 Grand Boutique Suite: *"The estate's principal room. Vaulted ceiling, private balcony."*
  - N° 04 Penthouse Suite: *"The whole top floor. A private rooftop. The longest sky in Mérida."*
- **Why it matters:** N° 02 ("Mornings stretch") and N° 04 ("The longest sky in Mérida") are *brilliant* one-liners — they're about an *experience*, a hour, a quality. N° 03 is the dud: "Vaulted ceiling, private balcony" is a real-estate listing. N° 01's "compact, considered, courtyard-adjacent" is alliteration substituting for image — "compact" + "considered" is the kind of pair-of-adjectives that sounds nice and tells you nothing about how the room *feels*.
- **Fix:** Rewrite the two laggards in the register of the two winners. Each room gets a *time*, a *sense*, or a *behavior*:
  - N° 01: *"The first room. You hear the courtyard before you see it."* (gives it a sense)
  - N° 03: *"The estate's principal room. The balcony over the Paseo, the bath you sit in too long."* (gives it a behavior — a thing you do in it)
  Four rooms = four distinct voices. Right now you have two voices and two listings.

### 6. Curated Mérida descriptions — the Honeymoon is the only one with prose teeth; the other three collapse to features — **MAJOR**
- **What:**
  - Honeymoon: *"Private breakfast in the courtyard. A cenote at dawn. A photographer for a single hour. The kind of week you remember out of order."* (excellent — the last line is the page's best sentence)
  - Cenotes & Haciendas: *"A private driver, a swim through limestone, lunch in a working henequen estate. Drive slowly. Return for siesta."* (good — has rhythm)
  - In-Suite Wellness: *"Mayan-trained therapists at your door. Copal, cacao, cool stone. Nothing rushed, nothing leaving the room."* (OK but reads as spa-menu — *what* is "nothing leaving the room" supposed to evoke? Privacy? Then say it)
  - Private Transfers: *"A car waiting before you land. A driver who waits without checking the time. 15 minutes from Manuel Crescencio Rejón."* (good)
- **Why it matters:** "The kind of week you remember out of order" is the only place on the page where the writer trusts the reader enough to land on a *resonance* rather than a *description*. The other three never reach that altitude. Wellness in particular reads like a Four Seasons booklet — "Mayan-trained therapists" with "copal, cacao, cool stone" is a list of ingredients; the kicker should not be an HR-style boundary ("nothing leaving the room") but a sensation or a behavior.
- **Fix:** Each curated card needs one closing line that earns the Honeymoon's standard. Try:
  - Wellness: *"…cool stone. You will not be the same shape afterward."*
  - Transfers: *"…without checking the time. The first thing you smell when the door opens is rain on limestone, even when it isn't raining."*
  - Cenotes: this one is fine; leave it.

### 7. The Art Collaboration manifesto — strong opening, but smuggles in marketing tics — **MAJOR**
- **What:** *"Treehouse is a curatorial concept built for spaces that already have a heartbeat. We partnered with SoHo Galleries in Mérida to seed Boutique with a living collection — drawings, ceramics, photography, the occasional uncomfortable thing — that turns over four times a year. Guests live inside the exhibition rather than walking through it. Some pieces are for sale. Most are not. None of them are for the lobby."*
- **Why it matters:** "The occasional uncomfortable thing" and "None of them are for the lobby" are perfect — they have *editorial* voice, the kind a real curator writes. But "a curatorial concept built for spaces that already have a heartbeat" is the kind of consultancy-deck phrasing the rest of the section is trying to escape. It's the only place in the manifesto where you hear a brand strategist instead of a curator. Also, "to seed Boutique with a living collection" — *seed* is start-up vocabulary; *living collection* is museum-board vocabulary; neither belongs in the same sentence.
- **Fix:** Open with a curator's voice, not a positioning statement. Rewrite the first sentence: *"Treehouse curates for places that aren't museums. A hotel, a restaurant, a private courtyard — the rooms have a job, and the art has to share them."* Then the rest stands. Specifically: replace "to seed Boutique with" → "We installed a rotating collection at Boutique" (active, plain, less corporate). And the closing — "None of them are for the lobby" — is the best line; reward it by giving it its own paragraph or line break.

### 8. Current Rotation deck — generic where it should be the most specific — **MAJOR**
- **What:** *"Rotation N° IV — Calor Lento. On view through the Yucatán summer. Six artists. Twenty-three works. Installed across the courtyard, the corridors, and the four suite vestibules."*
- **Why it matters:** *Calor Lento* (slow heat) is a beautiful Spanish title — and the deck never **says what the rotation is about**. A real gallery website would tell you the curatorial premise in a sentence: "Six artists thinking about heat as a slow architect of the Yucatán." Instead the deck only does an inventory ("six artists, twenty-three works") which is the same level of detail as the marquee underneath it. The deck is duplicating, not amplifying.
- **Fix:** Make the deck *curatorial*, not logistical. Move the inventory to the marquee/metadata (already there). Rewrite:
  *"Rotation N° IV — Calor Lento. Six artists working in and against the Yucatán summer — drawings made in the hour the courtyard refuses to cool, ceramics fired in henequen ash, a photographer who only shoots between two and four. On view through August."*
  Now the section has weight.

### 9. The interstitial Spanish pull-quote is a translation, not a quote — **MAJOR**
- **What:** *"Un refugio en el corazón de Mérida." — A sanctuary in the limestone heart of the city.*
- **Why it matters:** A Mexican reader will read the Spanish first and notice it's a stock phrase — *"un refugio en el corazón de Mérida"* is the literal default real-estate-listing tagline you'd see on any Centro property's brochure. There's nothing in it. The English subtitle is *richer* than the Spanish original ("limestone heart" is a great image). The bilingual move backfires: the Spanish reads thin to anyone who speaks it, and the English does the work. **Spanish-as-decoration**, not Spanish-as-meaning.
- **Fix:** Either drop the English subtitle (let the Spanish stand if it stands) OR rewrite the Spanish to carry the same image as the English: *"Un refugio en el corazón de cal."* (a sanctuary in the limestone heart) — now the Spanish carries the metaphor and the English subtitle is a clean translation, not a rescue. Better still, find the *idiomatic Mexican phrase* — ask a Yucateco reader; "corazón de cal" is unusual enough to feel intentional. Aphoristic, short, and **the Spanish leads the meaning**.

### 10. Footer Spanish coda — "Un refugio. Una casa. Quince llaves." — works, but it's the third tricolon in 30 seconds — **MAJOR**
- **What:** Footer: *"Un refugio. Una casa. Quince llaves."*
- **Why it matters:** As a single line in isolation, it's clean and good. But by the time the reader reaches the footer they've absorbed: hero tagline as tricolon, sanctuary opening as tricolon, sanctuary materials as tricolon, hero meta as tricolon (15/1909/21°N), Mas-Girbau-style pillars as tricolon, pull-quote as bilingual tricolon, and now this. The site has one rhythm. It chants. "Quiet luxury" doesn't chant — it *varies*. The Largo's signature is that it uses long sentences AND short fragments — restraint is partly about not always cutting to the same beat.
- **Fix:** Keep the footer line — it's the right place for a coda. But break the rhythm somewhere upstream so the chant doesn't get monotonous. Specifically: the Sanctuary paragraph should NOT end in a tricolon (see Finding 1 fix); the curated cards should not all be tricolons (Honeymoon and Cenotes & Haciendas are; Wellness is; Transfers is). Vary the cadence so the footer feels like an arrival, not a continuation.

### 11. Testimonials — four are excellent; one is generic — **MAJOR**
- **What:**
  - "We arrived for three nights and rearranged a flight to stay four. The house has a way of asking you to be still." — L & M · Brooklyn (excellent, specific behavior)
  - "The Penthouse rooftop at the last light of day, and a piece of art on the wall I quietly priced before I went to sleep." — Cécile · Paris (excellent — has a specific guilty pleasure)
  - "Fifteen rooms, and somehow they remembered we take our coffee differently. That is not service. That is attention." — Andrés · CDMX (excellent — the line "That is not service. That is attention." is sharp)
  - "A cenote at dawn, mezcal at sunset, and absolutely nobody else in the courtyard. I cannot recommend a hotel more honestly." — Iris · Copenhagen (good)
  - **"A house that has chosen what it wants to be. Refined, restored, unhurried." — Travel Diary · 2026** (this is the dud)
- **Why it matters:** "Refined, restored, unhurried" is a tricolon of marketing-deck adjectives — the same words the brand would use about itself. No real human writes about a hotel like this. And "Travel Diary · 2026" as the attribution is the only attribution without a name and a city; it reads as "we couldn't find a fifth real review so we wrote one." It's also tonally limp — *every other testimonial has a specific moment or behavior*, and this one has none.
- **Fix:** Either delete it (four strong voices > five with a dud) or rewrite it to match the others' specificity:
  *"I left two books behind on purpose, so we'd have to come back." — J. R. · São Paulo*
  Or:
  *"My husband does not nap. He napped here. Twice." — Mariana · Monterrey*
  The Andrés CDMX one is the standard. Hit it or cut.

### 12. CTAs — partially fixed; one still corporate — **MAJOR**
- **What:** The CTAs on the page:
  - Hero primary: "Reserve your stay"
  - Hero secondary: "Begin the tour"
  - Room cards: "Reserve this room →" / "Reserve this suite →" / "Reserve the penthouse →"
  - Location: "Open in maps →"
  - Art: "Book a curator-led walkthrough →"
  - Footer form: "Enquire"
  - Nav: "Reserve"
- **Why it matters:** "Begin the tour" is a tour-bus phrase. "Begin." alone (as the brief suggested) is far better — it's a single-word imperative that matches the cinematic restraint. "Enquire" is correct British-luxury vocabulary but slightly mannered for a Mexican boutique; "Write the house" or "Send a note" would be more *this hotel*. "Reserve your stay" is functional but doesn't say anything about *this* hotel — every Marriott has this CTA.
- **Fix:**
  - Hero primary: keep "Reserve your stay" OR sharpen to *"Reserve a key"* (echoes the "fifteen keys" line throughout)
  - Hero secondary: *"Begin"* (single word, matches the hush)
  - Form submit: *"Write the house"* or *"Send a note"* (not "Enquire")
  - Room CTAs: instead of three near-identical "Reserve this room/suite/penthouse," try each room's CTA as its own micro-line: *"Reserve N° 01"*, *"Reserve N° 02"*, *"Reserve N° 03"*, *"Reserve the top floor"* — preserves the numbering motif that runs through the whole page.

### 13. Location section — "Mérida's Champs-Élysées in a more honest mood" is *almost* great, but the qualifier patronizes — **MINOR**
- **What:** *"Boutique by The Museo sits at the head of Paseo de Montejo, Mérida's Champs-Élysées in a more honest mood."*
- **Why it matters:** "More honest mood" is doing two contradictory things: it (a) compares Mérida to Paris (which the city's tourism board has done for a hundred years, and which sophisticated Mérida lovers have come to mildly resent) and (b) gently sneers at Paris to seem cool. Both moves are smaller than the city deserves. The Largo's Porto copy never compares Porto to Lisbon or Barcelona — it lets Porto stand alone.
- **Fix:** Let Paseo de Montejo be itself. *"Boutique by The Museo sits at the head of Paseo de Montejo — Mérida's grand avenue, planted with laurels in the 1900s and never finished hurrying."* Or just: *"Boutique by The Museo sits at the head of Paseo de Montejo. The block has been quiet for a hundred years."* Both versions trust the reader to understand without the Paris crutch.

### 14. "20% off — the slow season" offer copy hits the wrong register — **MINOR**
- **What:** *"20% off — the slow season. June 1 — August 31, 2026. The city quiets, the courtyard cools after dark, and the house drops 20% on every room category. Honeymoons especially welcome."*
- **Why it matters:** Three things: (1) "20% off" in the H3 is correct e-commerce instinct but the wrong *register* for this site — every other H3 is a phrase, not a percentage. (2) "Drops 20% on every room category" is direct-response language: *drops*, *on every room category* — both are conversion copy, not house voice. (3) "Honeymoons especially welcome" is genuinely good — that's the line.
- **Fix:** Move the discount mechanic to the fine print where it belongs, and let the H3 hold the *story* of the slow season:
  *H3: "The slow season."*
  *Body: "June through August, the city quiets. The courtyard cools after dark, the rotations breathe, and the house opens at twenty percent less. Honeymoons especially welcome."*
  *Fine: "Use code VERANO26 · Minimum 3 nights · Jun 1 — Aug 31, 2026"*
  Now the offer is told as a season, not a sale.

### 15. Hero meta strip — "1909 · Estate" is interesting; "21°N · Yucatán" is filler — **MINOR**
- **What:** *15 Rooms · 1909 Estate · 21°N Yucatán*
- **Why it matters:** "15 Rooms" + "1909 Estate" each tell you something concrete. "21°N · Yucatán" is geo-decoration — *every* place in southern Mexico is around 21°N; it could be Cancun, Campeche, Veracruz. The number tells the buyer nothing specific about Mérida. It looks like an instrument-panel detail (which the brief wanted), but the data point is empty.
- **Fix:** Replace with a meta point that actually distinguishes Mérida or this property:
  - *"42m · Palacio Cantón"* (distance — matches the "block from Palacio Cantón" line)
  - *"90m · Paseo de Montejo"* (distance to the avenue)
  - *"VII · Calle 43"* (echoes the nav dot label)
  Any of these has *information density*. "21°N" has none.

### 16. "El Refugio" / "Las Habitaciones" / "La Mérida Curada" / "El Sitio" / "Las Voces" / "Las Ofertas" / "La Rotación Actual" — Spanish section eyebrows are pure decoration — **MINOR**
- **What:** Every section eyebrow is the Spanish article-noun for the section's English name.
- **Why it matters:** This is **Spanish as ornamentation** — exactly what the brief warns against. *El Refugio* over "Sanctuary," *Las Habitaciones* over "Rooms," *Las Voces* over "Voices" — none of these add meaning; they're a translation of the label below. A Mexican reader sees this as a tic. The only one that *adds* meaning is *La Rotación Actual* (Current Rotation), and that's because it's a piece of art-world vocabulary.
- **Fix:** Either commit to Spanish as the *primary* eyebrow and let English carry the section H2 (then the bilingual move becomes structural, not decorative), or use the Spanish slot to add information the English doesn't have. Examples:
  - Rooms: instead of *"Las Habitaciones"*, try *"Cuatro llaves"* (four keys — echoes the "fifteen keys" motif and tells the reader something)
  - Voices: instead of *"Las Voces"*, try *"Notas dejadas al salir"* (notes left at checkout — which is *already* in the section label below, just unify the two)
  - Sanctuary: instead of *"El Refugio"*, try *"La casa, despacio"* (the house, slowly)
  At minimum: the section's existing label "Las Voces — Notes left at checkout" is the right pattern. Promote that style to all the eyebrows.

### 17. Captions on the imagery are doing one job; could do two — **NIT**
- **What:** Sanctuary caption: *"N° 01 · La Casa"*. That's the only image caption in the document. Most photos run captionless.
- **Why it matters:** Captions are luxury copy's best understated weapon — they let the writer *narrate* without writing paragraphs. The single caption you have ("N° 01 · La Casa") is the most generic possible — a number and the most-used noun on the page. The art pieces have good caption metadata. The photo strip has none. The hero, breath quote, location image, voices background — all uncaptioned.
- **Fix:** Either commit to a caption convention site-wide (every panel image gets a "N° XX · [specific noun]" — and the specific noun is *not* "La Casa" but a particular thing — "El Patio al mediodía," "Madera del 1909," "El balcón sobre el Paseo") OR delete the lone sanctuary caption since it's an outlier. Right now it reads as a leftover from an earlier draft.

### 18. The breath quote attribution — "Field notes, January" — is the right move but unsigned — **NIT**
- **What:** *"Some houses ask to be admired. Others ask to be lived in slowly. This one is the second kind." — Field notes, January*
- **Why it matters:** "Field notes, January" is a *great* attribution — it implies a writer, a season, a notebook. But unsigned, it floats. Is this Treehouse's curator? A travel writer the hotel commissioned? The owner? The reader can't tell, and the ambiguity isn't artful — it's evasive. If you want this to feel like a *real* field-note from a real human, sign it: *"— J.R., Field notes, January 2026"* or attribute it to the curator: *"— T. Mendoza, Treehouse · Field notes."*
- **Fix:** Sign the field note. A specific initialed person makes it credible; the bare phrase makes it ad copy doing a costume change.

## What's working (coda)
- The Honeymoon's *"The kind of week you remember out of order"* is the page's best sentence — that's the register the whole site should aim for.
- The N° 02 room one-liner *"A sitting room. A second light. Mornings stretch."* is a model — three fragments, but the last one is a verb behavior, not a noun pile.
- *"Drive slowly. Return for siesta."* (Cenotes & Haciendas) is the right *behavioral* imperative for a luxury voice — gives the reader something to do, not just see.
- *"That is not service. That is attention."* (Andrés CDMX testimonial) reads like a real person who's slightly impatient with hotel-marketing language — perfect.
- The curator quote *"We didn't want the rooms to be backdrops for the art. We wanted the art to be the reason the rooms held still."* is editorially confident — that's the voice Treehouse needs throughout the section.
- The Resident's Allocation offer headline *"Six nights, slower."* is the right pattern — number + comma + adverb. Use that grammar more.

## Where to start, if only three findings get addressed

1. **Finding 3** (Pillars) — biggest differentiation upside; trades positioning labels for material-world anchors.

3. **Finding 9** (Interstitial Spanish) — single line, but the most visible "Spanish as decoration" moment on the page. Fix it and the bilingual register lifts site-wide.
