# V3 вЂ” Brutalist Art-Forward / CRITIQUE R2
## Focus: Copy, Narrative Voice, Content Density, Storytelling

R1 fixed the typography and apparatus. The page now *looks* like a catalog. R2 asks whether it *reads* like one вЂ” whether a single curator wrote every word, or whether hotel-marketing voice keeps slipping through the seams. Findings below are ordered by severity.

### 1. The manifesto is the page's thesis statement, and four of its six lines are clichГ©s. **CRITICAL**

**What:**
```
FIFTEEN ROOMS.
ONE COURTYARD.
NO COMPROMISE.
A REFUGE,
NOT A RESORT.
whisper, don't shout.
```

**Why it matters:** A manifesto in a brutalist art catalog earns its name by being *irreducible* вЂ” statements that could only be made by *this* house. Here, three of the six lines are quantitative restatements of facts that appear (again) in the data grid, the counters, and the landing tagline ("FIFTEEN ROOMS. / ONE COURTYARD."). One is a luxury-hotel clichГ© that has been on every boutique landing page since 2014 ("NO COMPROMISE."). One is a binary that flatters by negation ("A REFUGE, NOT A RESORT.") вЂ” also generic; every small hotel in the world refuses to call itself a resort. Only "whisper, don't shout." attempts a real position, and the design (rightly) demotes it visually because the line above it ("NOT A RESORT.") is doing the same anti-positioning move. The manifesto reads like a slogan deck, not a declaration of intent.

**Fix:** Replace at least three lines with specific, falsifiable, *only-here* declarations. Sample rewrite:

```
FIFTEEN ROOMS.
ONE COURTYARD.
NO LOBBY MUSIC.
THE ART STAYS FOR A SEASON.
THE FAMILY STAYS FOR GOOD.
whisper, don't shout.
```

Now line 3 names what the house refuses (lobby music вЂ” a brutalist particularity Peter & Paul would write). Line 4 commits the house to its programming. Line 5 quietly references owner-occupancy (the RamГ­rez family from the data cell), giving the manifesto a *who*. The whisper line lands as the punchline of a series that has earned a register shift, not as the only specific thing in the room.

### 2. The whisper line is a punchline without a setup. **CRITICAL**

**What:** `whisper, don't shout.` вЂ” rendered Cormorant italic, lowercase, ink-faded, after five lines of Inter 900 condensed all-caps.

**Why it matters:** R1 made the line *visually* quiet, which is correct typography. But the line is doing two contradictory things at once: it's the climactic position (line 6), it's the only register-shifted line, and yet it's also telling the reader to whisper. The result is a meta-statement that the page itself is whispering вЂ” and meta-statements are an art-school move, not a curatorial one. A curator wouldn't tell you they're whispering; they'd whisper. Compare to Peter & Paul's voice: P&P would never write "whisper, don't shout." вЂ” they'd just write something quiet and leave the inference to the reader.

The line also re-treads "NOT A RESORT." on a different axis (volume, instead of category). Two anti-positioning lines in a six-line manifesto is one too many.

**Fix:** Either (a) cut the line and let the manifesto end on a specific, declarative final beat вЂ” strongest move; or (b) replace with a Cormorant-italic specific image that *demonstrates* quiet rather than naming it. Sample: `coffee at six. limestone, before the light.` (steals the strong image from the coda, where it's currently buried). The whisper happens in the descriptive concreteness, not in being told. If you keep the metacommentary, demote it from line 6 to a tiny mono footer beneath the coda вЂ” out of the climax position.

### 3. The curatorial statement uses curator-shaped sentences but doesn't make a curator's argument. **CRITICAL**

**What:**
> The hotel is the gallery. Fifteen rooms, one courtyard, and a rotating slate of work installed where one ordinarily expects only a luggage rack and a thermostat. *El JardГ­n interior* gathers six Yucatecan and Latin-American artists whose practice circles the same quiet question: what does it mean to make a private interior public?
>
> Works are installed throughout the property вЂ” above the breakfast table, beside the bathtub, at the foot of bed в„–&nbsp;7 вЂ” and rotate at the close of every fourth lunar month. Guests live inside the show. The catalog ships at checkout.

**Why it matters:** Diagnostic test: extract the *thesis* вЂ” what is this exhibition arguing? "What does it mean to make a private interior public?" is a question, not a thesis. The statement describes the premise (hotel-as-gallery), the install logic (works in rooms), and the schedule (lunar months), but it never tells you what *El JardГ­n interior* claims about the world. A real curatorial statement has a position. Compare any wall text at the Tate or the Whitney: *the artists in this exhibition argue that X*; *seen together, these works reveal Y*. Here, the artists are gathered, but the curator never says what the gathering shows.

The "luggage rack and thermostat" detail and the "bed в„– 7" specificity are the best sentences on the page вЂ” they have texture. But the conceptual frame ("what does it mean to make a private interior public?") is undergraduate-art-history filler.

**Fix:** Add one sentence that does the curatorial work. Sample insertion after the question mark:

> Each of the six works was made for, or relocated into, a domestic space the artist could not own: a borrowed kitchen, a hotel room, a friend's stairwell. *El JardГ­n interior* is the show that argues a domestic interior is not the opposite of a public one вЂ” it is a public one we have agreed to call private.

Now there's a position. The artists are doing one thing, not just "circling a question." (Even if you don't use this exact thesis вЂ” pick one. The exhibition needs a claim.)

Also: "the close of every fourth lunar month" is a phrase that sounds curatorial but means *every four months*, which is just *quarterly*, which is what the program's footer already says ("Rotates quarterly"). Pick one register вЂ” either lunar months for poetry or quarterly for clarity. Doing both reads as a curator caught dressing up plain language.

### 4. The Spanish is stylized as label-stuffing, not load-bearing. **MAJOR**

**What:** ALL-CAPS Spanish appears as: `MANIFIESTO`, `ESPECIFICACIONES`, `LA COLECCIГ“N`, `LA EXPOSICIГ“N`, `MГ‰RIDA CURADA`, `NOTAS DE CAMPO`, `ГЌNDICE`, `EL ESPACIO`, `SECCIONES / SECTIONS`, `ORIGEN / PERГЌODO / SUPERFICIE / VISTA / CAMA / BAГ‘O / OCC. MAX / DESDE`, `PATIO INTERIOR`, `02 ADULTOS`, `DESDE $285 USD / NOCHE`, `LINOS DE LINO`, `SALA`, `TERRAZA`, `CERO`, `ROOMS / LLAVES`, `COURTYARD / PATIO`, `CHAINS / CADENAS`.

**Why it matters:** A real bilingual MГ©rida hotel doesn't translate "ROOMS" to "LLAVES" вЂ” *llaves* is "keys" (as in physical room keys), which is a charming brutalist move *if* it appeared once with intent. Repeated across counters, room specs, and section labels, the Spanish flips between literal translation ("ROOMS / LLAVES") and stylized synonym ("LLAVES" used for "rooms") without rule. `CAMA / LINOS DE LINO` translates to "BED / LINEN LINENS" вЂ” a tautology in Spanish that an actual Yucateco copywriter would never write. `02 ADULTOS` and `04 ADULTOS` use leading zeros for visual rhythm; fine in mono apparatus, but the rest of the page mixes leading-zero ("04 NIGHTS / SUITE в„– III") with no-leading-zero ("STAY: 06 NIGHTS / PENTHOUSE") with letter-spelled ("CERO") without rule.

A reader fluent in Spanish reads these labels and clocks them as American-designer-bilingual-styling, not MГ©rida-native voice. That's a credibility wound on a page that depends on being credible about MГ©rida.

**Fix:** Three rules, applied consistently:
1. Use Spanish where it does *work* (terms of art, place names, materials with no clean English equivalent): `chukum`, `henequГ©n`, `patio`, `hacienda`, `Paseo de Montejo`, `cenote`. Keep the section-header bilingual conceits (`MANIFIESTO вЂ” DECLARATION OF INTENT`, `LA EXPOSICIГ“N вЂ” TREEHOUSE Г— SOHO GALLERIES`) вЂ” these earn the dual register.
2. Cut the false-friend translations: `ROOMS / LLAVES` в†’ just `ROOMS, 15`. `CHAINS / CADENAS` в†’ `INDEPENDENT.` `COURTYARD / PATIO` в†’ just `PATIO` (it's a real English-language loanword for this typology).
3. Fix the room specs: `LINOS DE LINO` в†’ `KING / LINO BELGA` (or just `LINEN, BELGIAN`). `SALA` and `BAГ‘O` are fine as Spanish labels because the *building typology* is Mexican.

The rule: Spanish for what is Mexican; English for what is universal. Right now Spanish is a finish, not a substance.

### 8. Field Notes prose is *too* clean вЂ” five guests with the same prose rhythm. **MAJOR**

**What:** Five review-card texts. Sentence-by-sentence rhythm across all five:
- M.H.: short / declarative / declarative.
- L. & R.: declarative / declarative / declarative-with-period-clause.
- A.K.: declarative / declarative / declarative-with-period-clause.
- J.P.: one-word sentence ("Honeymoon.") then four short declaratives.
- C.D.: balanced compound sentence, then declarative.

**Why it matters:** Real guest writing has tics вЂ” exclamation points, "honestly," "btw," misspellings, breathless run-ons, the awkward sentence that gives away a non-native speaker, a verb-tense slip mid-paragraph. These five reviews all read like the *same writer* doing five plausible voices. The brief asks: "do reviews sound like guests or fabricated marketing?" вЂ” these read as a copywriter doing tasteful approximations of guest writing, which is the *exact* register a Peter & Paul reader has been trained to clock as inauthentic.

Specific tells:
- All five end on punctuation-perfect period-clauses. No fragments. No questions.
- "The driver knew every back road." вЂ” a copywriter's reach for texture; a real guest writes "the driver knew where to go."
- "The hush of limestone walls before sunrise" appears in the manifesto coda; "MГ©rida is loud at the right moments and silent at the right ones" (C.D.) is the same rhythm. The reviews and the curator are using the same prose hand.
- "The art is not decoration. There is a Pech piece in the courtyard..." (L. & R.) вЂ” a real guest in Portugal would not, in a review, drop a Yucatecan artist's surname mid-sentence with no honorific. They'd say "a sculpture in the courtyard."

**Fix:** Rewrite at least three of five with deliberate guest-prose imperfection. Sample:

> **в„– 041 В· 12 MAR 2026** вЂ” "We arrived stressed from the airport and within an hour I'd forgotten what I was stressed about. Fifteen rooms is exactly the right number вЂ” anything bigger and the spell breaks. (Coffee at 6am, on the dot, every morning. The owners are obsessive in the right way.)" вЂ” M.H. / SAN FRANCISCO, CA

> **в„– 038 В· 04 FEB 2026** вЂ” "Honest review вЂ” we came because of the architecture and stayed an extra two nights because of the sculpture in the courtyard. I don't normally notice art in hotels. This one I couldn't stop looking at. We took the catalog home and our friends ask about it." вЂ” L. & R. / LISBOA, PT

> **в„– 029 В· 21 NOV 2025** вЂ” "The cenote day was the best thing we did in Mexico. Three cenotes, no other tourists, lunch under a tree at an old hacienda. I keep telling people about it." вЂ” A.K. / LONDON, UK

Notice: the new versions have parentheticals, "honest review," ungrammatical em-dashes, a casual "Mexico" instead of "MГ©xico," and a sentence fragment ("Three cenotes, no other tourists, lunch under a tree at an old hacienda."). Real guest writing isn't sloppy вЂ” it just isn't *polished*. The brutalist apparatus around the reviews (ledger stamp, mono attribution) is strong enough to elevate looser prose; right now the prose is over-fitted to the apparatus.
